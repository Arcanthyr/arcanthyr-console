# Reckoning of Time
[DOMAIN: Tasmanian Criminal Law] [CATEGORY: doctrine] [TYPE: doctrine] [TOPIC: Statutory Interpretation of Time Periods] [CONCEPTS: reckoning, exclusion of days, sentencing orders, authority, time calculation] [CITATION: hoc-b001-m003-reckoning-of-time] [ACT: Acts Interpretation Act 1931] [CASE: Prowse v McIntyre; Forster v Jododex Australia Pty Ltd]

Tasmanian statutory interpretation requires you to exclude the day the period is dated from when you reckon a period "dated or reckoned from a given day." Section 29 of the Acts Interpretation Act 1931 (Tas) states that a period reckoned from a given day "shall be reckoned exclusively of that day" — so the first day is excluded from the calculation.

NOTE that sentencing orders usually say "commencing on," so it would be inclusive of the same day, as it is expressed by the court. If it were to say "from x date," then it would be the next day. Confusing. Look at the Acts Interpretation Act.

Defendants are usually given full credit for parts of days served. Supported by consistent authority in the reckoning of time cases: Prowse v McIntyre [1961] Tas SR 9 (regarding the exclusion of the first day unless a statute indicates otherwise).

The general principle of law is that the law is not concerned with divisions of a day, and fractions of a day are disregarded: Prowse v McIntyre [1961] HCA 79; [1961] 111 CLR 264; Forster v Jododex Australia Pty Ltd [1972] HCA 61; [1972] 127 CLR 421 at 446, per Gibbs J. In Forster, Gibbs J referred to Sir William Grant MR in Lester v Garland (1808) 15 VES Jun, at 257 (33 ER, at 752).

# Legal Standards for Consent to Search
[DOMAIN: Tasmanian Criminal Law] [CATEGORY: doctrine] [TYPE: doctrine] [TOPIC: not resisting or not saying no does not amount to consent] [CONCEPTS: consent, search, reasonable belief, lawful entry, privacy rights] [CITATION: hoc-b002-m001-legal-standards-for-consent] [ACT: Misuse of Drugs Act] [CASE: Tasmania v Hodges [2022] TASSC 45]

Not resisting or not saying no to a search does not amount to consent. This statement underlines the crucial legal standard that consent for searches must be unequivocal and that mere non-resistance does not equate to giving permission. The case of *Tasmania v Hodges* confirms this notion by emphasizing the distinction between compliance and actual consent, highlighting the necessity for an affirmative act or clear demonstration of consent from the individual being searched. This aligns with broader principles in criminal law that seek to protect individual privacy rights against arbitrary state power, reaffirming that consent must be informed and voluntary.

In addition to ensuring valid consent, there are specific legal requirements surrounding the execution of search powers. The pre-condition to search powers on reasonable belief under the *Misuse of Drugs Act* is "lawful" entry to private premises. Lawful entry must involve a warrant or similar authority. Private premises are broadly construed to include places like hotel rooms, especially when the occupant has established their personal control over the space.

### 11. Reasonable Grounds for Suspicion
[DOMAIN: Tasmanian Criminal Law] [ACT: N/A] [SECTION: N/A] [CITATION: Lindley v Rutter - Reasonable Grounds] [CATEGORY: procedure] [TYPE: annotation] [TOPIC: Reasonable Suspicion Standards] [CONCEPTS: reasonable suspicion, legal standards, Lindley v Rutter, police authority, search justification]  
***3.4 Grounds of the Search: Reasonable Grounds to Suspect or Believe.***  
***What is reasonable suspicion?*** ***Lindley v Rutter*** \[1981\] QB 128. A drunken woman was taken to a police station where her bra was taken off. She was arrested -- resisted search -- two officers searched her believing they were under a standing order to search every female prisoner and remove the bra for their own protection. 

There is a duty of an officer to have a lawful purpose for search -- a standing order from a senior officer is not enough. There has to be a basis. Held: If the officer's mind did not apply itself to having a lawful purpose for the search, then the search was unlawful (subjective mindset of officer required level of analysis). There was no legal basis to search her -- standing order not sufficient.

# Purpose of Search Warrants at Common Law
[DOMAIN: Tasmanian Criminal Law] [CATEGORY: doctrine] [TYPE: doctrine] [TOPIC: purpose of a search warrant] [CONCEPTS: search warrant, common law, judicial oversight, occupiers' rights, trespass] [CITATION: hoc-b003-m001-purpose-of-search-warrants] [ACT: None] [CASE: George v Rockett (1990) 170 CLR 104]

A search warrant operates as a control design -- it imposes an independent judicial officer between official and citizen. This is why at common law (CL), we see the initial conservative approach to issuing search warrants and the jealously with which CL protects the rights of occupiers. At CL, it is necessary to ensure that goods seized during the search were covered by the search warrant; if not, the seizing officers could be sued for trespass to private property. The common law rejected the idea of general warrants, which are unspecified warrants that allow searches at any time or any place, because such warrants had been used by the Star Chamber in a very oppressive way. 

In the High Court case ***George v Rockett*** (1990) 170 CLR 104, the purpose of a search warrant was explained, highlighting its role in controlling state power over private individuals. The fundamental purpose of a search warrant is to protect the rights of citizens from arbitrary state actions, ensuring that searches are conducted under the authority of law and with proper oversight. This conservatism reflects a core respect for individual rights against arbitrary state intrusions, serving to balance law enforcement needs with the protections guaranteed to citizens.

# Search and Seizure of Mail Under the Australian Postal Corporation Act
[DOMAIN: Tasmanian Criminal Law] [CATEGORY: doctrine] [TYPE: doctrine] [TOPIC: Police can receive mail without a warrant but cannot open it] [CONCEPTS: search warrant, illicit drugs, prescribed belief, chain of custody, evidence] [CITATION: hoc-b004-m001-search-seizure-mail] [ACT: Australian Postal Corporation Act 1989 (Cth)] [CASE: None]

Per s 90N Australian Postal Corporation Act 1989 (the APCA)

**Non-Negotiables (Hard Rules)**

**1. Police can RECEIVE mail without a warrant --- but not open it**

- Australia Post can lawfully **hand over** suspicious mail if it's evidence of an offence.

- **Taking possession ≠ searching or opening.**

- Opening **always** requires a warrant (Search Warrants Act s 5).

**2. Illicit drugs are NOT "dangerous or injurious" for postal law**

- You **cannot** rely on APCA s 90Z to open mail just because it contains drugs.

- Courts have been explicit: drugs ≠ explosives / immediate danger.

- This is a **repeat failure point** in prosecutions.

**3. You must form YOUR OWN prescribed belief**

- A Post employee's suspicion **is not enough**.

- You must personally meet **s 29 Misuse of Drugs Act** threshold.

- "Prescribed belief" = more than suspicion, less than proof (George v Rockett).

- Common lawful method: **drug dog indication** you personally observe.

**4. Even with prescribed belief --- you STILL need a warrant to open**

- s 29 MDA allows **seizure**, not opening.

- Opening without a warrant = unlawful search, high exclusion risk.

**What Australia Post CAN and CAN'T Do**

**They can:**

- X-ray, sniff, scan (non-intrusive examination).

- Disclose the article to police if it's suspected evidence.

- Act under APCA protections if done lawfully and in good faith.

**They cannot:**

- Open mail unless very specific exceptions apply (undeliverable, quarantine, etc.).

- Create lawful grounds for police just by saying "we suspect drugs".

**Practical Workflow (Defensible Path)**

1. **Confirm permitted person**

   - Verify the AusPost employee is lawfully authorised.

2. **Document how the parcel came to attention**

   - Random scan vs targeted activity matters (Melick issue).

3. **Independently form prescribed belief**

   - Drug dog, leakage, weight inconsistencies, smell, etc.

   - You must be able to articulate this **personally**.

4. **Seize but do not open**

   - Secure chain of custody.

5. **Obtain a warrant**

   - Telephone warrant acceptable.

   - Can rely on AusPost info + your own observations.

6. **Open only after warrant**

   - Anything earlier risks exclusion.

**Key Risk Triggers (Red Flags)**

- Opening mail "because Post said so"

- Treating drugs as "dangerous goods"

- No record of *how* belief was formed

- Post employees exceeding their authority (x-ray misuse, disclosures)

- Assuming urgency removes warrant requirements

**Bottom Line**

- **Mail is one of the easiest ways to lose an otherwise strong drug matter.**

- If you shortcut belief formation or opening authority, **the evidence dies**.

- This policy is conservative by design --- follow it and prosecutions survive.

# Provision of Interpreter and Legal Context for Searches
[DOMAIN: Tasmanian Criminal Law] [CATEGORY: doctrine] [TYPE: doctrine] [TOPIC: police powers and provision of interpreters] [CONCEPTS: interpreter, police powers, search, common law, statutory authority] [CITATION: hoc-b005-m001-provision-of-interpreter-legal-context] [ACT: Criminal Law (Detention & Interrogation) Act 1995 (Tas), Police Offences Act 1935 (Tas), Forensic Procedures Act 2000 (Tas), Family Violence Act 2004 (Tas), Misuse of Drugs Act 2001 (Tas), Search Warrants Act 1997 (Tas)] [CASE: Tas v Stojakovic [2008] TASSC 48, DPP v Viel & Vocaj [2024] VCC 336, VtV v R [2012] VSCA 213, Lindley v Rutter [1981] QB 128, Nguyen v Elliot (1995), Coco v R [1994] 179 CLR 427, Kuru v New South Wales [2008] HCA 26]

Provided for under s5 of the Criminal Law (Detention & Interrogation) Act 1995 (Tas). The case of Tas v Stojakovic [2008] TASSC 48 discusses the right to an interpreter in custodial circumstances emphasizing the need for competent interpretation to ensure fair police interviews. Additionally, the Victorian case of DPP v Viel & Vocaj [2024] VCC 336 reaffirmed similar rights in the context of police interviews and the role of interpreters under the law.

The Tasmanian Police "interviewing guidelines" further stipulate that an interpreter must be obtained when necessary, reflecting broader principles concerning access to justice and effective communication during police procedures. It's pertinent to also refer to VtV v R [2012] VSCA 213 regarding hearsay implications in the context of needing to summons interpreters, underscoring the essential role of interpreters in ensuring the proceedings are conducted fairly and justly.

In regard to police powers of search, the *International Covenant on Civil and Political Rights* (Article 17) indicates the importance of privacy rights and restricts search powers to defined circumstances. The common law recognises the right to privacy and that interference with this right should only occur in extreme circumstances. At common law, lawful search and seizure can only take place pursuant to a warrant; incident to a lawful arrest; or with consent.

In *Lindley v Rutter* [1981] QB 128, the court held that the police lacked a lawful basis for a search that involved the removal of the accused's underwear, thereby allowing the appeal and quashing the convictions. The ruling reiterates that police cannot simply act on standing orders; they must assess each situation's particular facts to ascertain whether a search is warranted. The opinion clarified that police powers regarding searches incident to arrest only extend to:

1. Obtaining evidence.
2. Preventing the destruction of evidence.
3. Locating items that may assist the accused in escaping or committing further crime (e.g., finding a weapon).
4. Searching for items that may be used to inflict self-harm.

Thus, the court concluded that police search powers are limited to instances where there are specific reasons to search an individual, and general policies do not provide sufficient justification.

The principles enshrined above now hold statutory force, notably in the Police Offences Act 1935 (Tas.), s 58B, which lays out the circumstances under which police may search a person in custody. This includes clauses delineating the officer's reasonable grounds for searching, as well as the prohibited requirement for removal of clothing unless justified.

In *Nguyen v Elliot* (1995), the police action was scrutinized regarding the search of a group of Asian youths displaying suspicious behavior around police. The court ultimately held that there was no reasonable basis for suspicion, vindicating the accused despite police advocacy for the need for action based on prior behavior. The court was hesitant but underscored the necessity of reasonable belief for effective action by the police.

Moreover, substantial limitations on search powers are seen in the *Misuse of Drugs Act* 2001 (Tas) and the *Crimes Act* 1914 (Cth), which outline distinct provisions for searches related to drug offenses and more general policing activities.

Medical procedures also have distinct statutory frameworks such as outlined in the *Forensic Procedures Act 2000* (Tas), which governs a wide spectrum of forensic sampling methods while distinguishing between intimate and non-intimate procedures. Non-intimate procedures may be executed with consent; also requiring a command from higher authority such as a senior police officer. In contrast, intimate procedures necessitate informed consent or must be ordered by a judicial officer.

The common law regarding searches of premises affords extensive protection. For instance, the importance of clear legislative authority for police entry into private property without consent was reiterated in *Coco v R* (1994) 179 CLR 427. This principle necessitates that any statutory authority for entering premises must be expressed in unmistakable language. At common law, searches without consent are circumscribed largely to those executed under warrant or incidental to lawful arrest.

In regards to family violence contexts, the Family Violence Act 2004 (Tas), specifically empowers police to enter premises under certain circumstances to prevent violence, emphasizing the proactive stance police are authorized to adopt in such urgent situations.

The recent case of *Kuru v New South Wales* [2008] HCA 26 further reinforces foundational principles around the necessity of obtaining consent or lawful authority for premises' entry, laying the groundwork for how police conduct can be evaluated legally in the context of search and seizure operations.

Your honour, both parties were apprehended today by officers of Tasmania Police. Your honour, the execution of interstate warrants is provided for under **PART 5** of that same Act. Section 83, sub-s (14), paragraph (a) empowers a justice of the peace to adjourn these proceedings and remand the defendants on bail, or in such custody as the justice of the peace specifies, for the period of the adjournment. It is my application that these proceedings be adjourned until 14:15 tomorrow, and that the defendants be held in remand, to appear by video link at that time. **Section 3: Interpretation**

***Magistrate***, except in sections 57 and 67, includes:

(a) a justice of the peace who has power to issue warrants under a law of the State in which the justice holds that office; and

(b) a person who is appointed under section 120 of the Magistrates' Court Act 1989 of Victoria as a bail justice, or is a bail justice because of holding a prescribed office within the meaning of section 121 of that Act.

***Person under restraint*** means a person who:

(a) is on bail; or

(b) has been conditionally released from prison (whether on parole, licence, work release, home detention, or otherwise) before the end of a term of imprisonment to which he or she has been sentenced; or

(c) is subject to the supervision of another person under a probation order; or

(d) is serving a period of home detention or a term of imprisonment by way of periodic detention; or

(e) is subject to:

(i) a community service order; or

(ii) a community-based order; or

(iii) an attendance order; or

(iv) a work and development order; or

(v) any other restriction on his or her movements, imposed by law or by order of a court, that is inconsistent with the person complying with a subpoena served on the person under this Act;

but does not include a person who is in prison.

# Arguments in Support of Voluntary and Intentional Actions
[DOMAIN: Tasmanian Criminal Law] [CATEGORY: doctrine] [TYPE: doctrine] [TOPIC: test of voluntary and intentional actions] [CONCEPTS: defendant, voluntary act, police communication, strict liability, absolute liability] [CITATION: hoc-b007-m001-voluntary-intentional-test] [ACT: None] [CASE: None]

Where defendant is clear, confident, deliberate in their denial/communication with police about their version of events, it is arguable on the facts that they were not presenting as someone who commits an involuntary act. 

A strict liability offence is one where "intent" does not need to be proved. However, the defence of honest and reasonable mistake is still available. Need only prove that the defendant performed the prohibited act. Driving without a licence is a strict liability offence. There is a defence if the driver has no notice of the fact that their licence has been suspended (case law and in defence prescribed in the Act).

Need not prove that the defendant knew the action was an offence, only that he committed it.

The test of absolute vs Strict: the words of the statute itself; the subject matter in which it is concerned; whether "absolute" liability will resolve the mischief concerned.

A crime which an intent to bring about a particular result is upon the true construction of the provisions of the code or statute creating the offence.

To further detail the distinction between strict and absolute liability, the law recognises three categories of statutory offences -- (1) those in which the prosecution must prove some state of mind, often referred to as mens rea, as an essential ingredient; (2) offences of "strict liability", as to which the prosecution need not prove any mental element unless the defendant adduces or elicits evidence suggesting an honest and reasonable mistaken belief in a state of facts that, if true, would render his or her acts or omissions innocent; and (3) offences of "absolute liability", as to which the prosecution does not ever need to prove any mental element but need only prove the objective ingredients of the offences: *He Kaw Teh v R* [1985] HCA 43; (1985) 157 CLR 523.

In *Proudman v Dayman* [1941] HCA 28; (1941) 67 CLR 536, the High Court was concerned with a charge of permitting an unlicensed person to drive a motor vehicle on a road. It was argued that the prosecutor needed to prove either knowledge that the driver was unlicensed or indifference. It was held that proof of knowledge or indifference was unnecessary. Rich ACJ and Dixon J (as he then was) held, on the facts, that no question of an honest and reasonable mistaken belief arose in that case. Dixon J suggested that that would have been a defence. At 541 he said:

"The burden of establishing honest and reasonable mistake is in the first place upon the defendant and he must make it appear that he had reasonable grounds for believing in the existence of a state of facts, which, if true, would take his act outside the operation of the enactment and that on those grounds he did so believe. The burden possibly may not finally rest upon him of satisfying the tribunal in case of doubt."

The question of what the Crown needs to prove as to the state of mind of a defendant in prosecutions under ss7, 8(1) and 9 has to be determined in accordance with the ordinary principles of statutory interpretation. There is a common law presumption that mens rea is an essential ingredient in every offence: *Sherras v De Rutzen* [1985] 1 QB 918 at 921; *He Kaw Teh v R* (above) per Gibbs CJ at 528. Mens rea was described in *Sherras v De Rutzen* by Wright J at 921 as "an evil intention, or a knowledge of the wrongfulness of the act". As Gibbs CJ made clear in *He Kaw Teh* at 529, one must consider the words of the statute creating the offence and the subject matter of the statute in order to determine whether the common law presumption has been displaced.

In broad terms, statutory offences fall into three categories if distinguished on the basis of the mental element involved in them. (1) Offences which include as an element that the accused had guilty knowledge or intent. (2) Offences which include as an element that the accused did not have an honest belief on reasonable grounds that the circumstances were such as to make his or her behaviour innocent. These are offences of strict liability. (3) Offences which do not include either of the above elements. These are offences of absolute liability.

# Unfitness to Stand Trial Under the Criminal Justice (Mental Impairment) Act 1999
[DOMAIN: Tasmanian Criminal Law] [CATEGORY: legislation] [TYPE: legislation] [TOPIC: criteria for unfitness to stand trial] [CONCEPTS: unfitness, trial, mental impairment, court proceedings, prosecution] [CITATION: hoc-b008-m001-unfitness-trial] [ACT: Criminal Justice (Mental Impairment) Act 1999] [CASE: None]

A person is unfit to stand trial for an offence if, because the person's mental processes are disordered or impaired or for any other reason, the person is --

(a) unable to understand the nature of the charge; or

(b) unable to plead to the charge or to exercise the right of challenge; or

(c) unable to understand the nature of the proceedings; or

(d) unable to follow the course of the proceedings; or

(e) unable to make a defence or answer the charge.

Notwithstanding subsection (1)(e), a person is not unfit to be tried if the only reason that the person is unable to make a defence or answer a charge is that he or she is suffering from memory loss.

A person is presumed fit to stand trial unless upon investigation it is established on the balance of probabilities that he or she is unfit. Such investigation can be commenced by the court, of its own initiative, by the defendant or by the prosecutor at any time after a person is charged. Before such investigation is undertaken it must appear to the court that there is a real and substantial question as to fitness to stand trial.

Therefore, if a prosecutor becomes aware of matters that would raise a question as to fitness to stand trial, there lies an obligation to raise it with the defendant's counsel or, if unrepresented, with the court. Ordinarily, the fitness issue will be raised by the defendant's counsel.

In these circumstances, a prosecutor should consider the following:

• whether there is a reasonable prospect of conviction on each or any of the charges

• whether a report has been obtained from a suitably qualified expert providing an opinion on the defendant's fitness to stand trial which addresses the criteria set out in s8

• whether the report addresses the likelihood of the defendant becoming fit to stand trial during the next 12 months

The real and substantial question as to a defendant's fitness to stand trial only arises if one of the five criteria laid out in s8 is reasonably open. The issue of whether a defendant is fit to stand trial should be left to a jury unless no reasonable jury, properly instructed, could find the accused was not fit to be tried. In some circumstances it will be readily apparent that the defendant is unfit to stand trial. However, where the issue of fitness is unclear an independent assessment may be required.

In determining whether it is appropriate to obtain an independent assessment of the defendant, a prosecutor should prepare a memorandum outlining the reasons for such a course of action. The consent of the Director or Deputy Director is required before an independent expert opinion is sought for that purpose. Where fitness to stand trial is an issue requiring investigation, the principles discussed by Smith J in R v Presser [1958] VR 45 at 48; [1958] ALR 248 cited with approval in Ngatayi v R (1980) 147 CLR 1 at 8, are of consequence when considering the criteria laid out in s8.

# Amendments to Complaints and Prejudice
[DOMAIN: Tasmanian Criminal Law] [CATEGORY: doctrine] [TYPE: doctrine] [TOPIC: amendment of complaints requires showing of prejudice] [CONCEPTS: prejudice, amendments, complaints, magistrate, cross-examination] [CITATION: hoc-b009-m001-amendments-complaints-prejudice] [ACT: Police Offences Act 1935] [CASE: Nilsson v McDonald]

The notion of prejudice in the context of amendments to complaints at the close of cases encompasses the capacity of a defendant to demonstrate that they would suffer detriment if the amendment were permitted. In *Nilsson v McDonald* [2009] TASSC 1 (16 January 2009), the Supreme Court appeal to allow an amendment at the end of the trial was denied. The magistrate had initially allowed the prosecutor's amendment, determining that the defendant could not substantiate any claim of prejudice that would ensue from the amendment. The defendant was given the opportunity to ask further questions, and it was evident from the case's development and the manner in which the defendant conducted their cross-examination that the case had been argued on the premise of the amendment sought. It was characterized as a minor amendment.

In *Nilsson*, the complaint identified the charge as "Fail to comply with a direction of a police officer," breaching "Section 15B(2) [Police Offences Act 1935]." During the hearing, the particulars were amended to specify: "You are charged with on the 15th June, 2007 at the Royal Hobart Hospital a public place at Hobart in Tasmania having been directed under the provisions of subsection 1, to leave that place and not return for a specified period of not less than 4 hours, by Constable PAJAK a police officer who had reasonable grounds for believing you had committed or was likely to commit an offence, [and/or has committed or is likely to commit a breach of the peace] and you unlawfully failed to comply with that direction." The magistrate, when permitting the amendment, was satisfied that there would be no prejudice to the applicant. He sought clarification from the applicant's counsel regarding any potential prejudice and pointed out that the evidence presented indicated a belief on the part of police officers that there had been or was likely to be a breach of peace, as alleged by the applicant. The magistrate asked what prejudice would arise if he allowed the applicant to further question witnesses on matters that might not have been covered. While the counsel for the applicant was caught off guard by the application, she could not articulate specific prejudice to her client.

In denying the review, the magistrate's conclusion was upheld that the amendment did not impair the applicant's position. The applicant's written submissions demonstrated a misunderstanding regarding the content requirements of a complaint. It was established that the charge remained that of failing to obey the direction of a police officer both before and after the amendment; only the basis for that direction was modified. The change was consistent with the evidential foundation, and counsel for the applicant had been adequately informed to cross-examine on the police officers' reasoning. No claims were made by the applicant's counsel that additional witnesses were necessary for a more comprehensive understanding of the applicant's behavior, nor did they request an adjournment for further evidence. As such, it was determined that the applicant faced no prejudice due to the amendments.

His Honour pointed out that the evidence led at the hearing demonstrated very clearly that the police officers believed, rightly or wrongly, that there had either already been a breach of the peace or there was likely to be one, having regard to the applicant's behaviour as asserted by them. He also pointed out that the cross-examination by counsel had been directed to those issues. His Honour enquired what prejudice the applicant would suffer if, in particular, he permitted her to question witnesses further about any matters that she believed had not been covered already. While admittedly, counsel for the applicant was apparently taken by surprise by the application, she was unable to identify any particular prejudice which might flow to her client. In those circumstances his Honour permitted the amendment.*

\[37\] *The applicant's written submissions again highlighted a misconception as to what a complaint needed to contain. It was not necessary for the complaint to identify the nature of the offence which might have been said to have been committed. The charge against the applicant was, both prior to and following the amendment, that of failing to obey the direction of a police officer. What was altered by the amendment was the addition of a basis upon which a police officer may have given that direction. The alteration was entirely consistent with the evidence, the applicant's counsel and the applicant had heard all the evidence, and the applicant's counsel had cross-examined in detail and at length in respect of the reasons the police officer gave for giving a direction to the applicant. There was no suggestion by the applicant's counsel that she could have or would have called other witnesses as to the nature of the applicant's conduct. In fact, that suggestion was only raised in submissions on the hearing of the review. The learned magistrate heard evidence from other witnesses present at the time of the incidents involving the applicant. There was no application from the applicant's counsel for an adjournment to enable her to present evidence from any other persons who might have been in the vicinity and, indeed, the applicant did not identify any specific witnesses who might have been able to give relevant and cogent evidence.*

\[38\] *I am not satisfied the applicant was in any way prejudiced by the amendment permitted by the learned magistrate. I am satisfied the learned magistrate did not make an error in permitting the amendment and this ground of review should fail.*

### Presumptions for and against Joinder
[DOMAIN: Tasmanian Criminal Law] [ACT: Criminal Code] [SECTION: Section 311] [CITATION: Criminal Code s 311 - Presumptions for Joinder] [CATEGORY: procedure] [TYPE: legal doctrine] [TOPIC: Presumptions in Joinder of Counts] [CONCEPTS: joinder, charges, Tasmanian law, offence, presumption]

Discussion on legal principles relating to the joinder of offences under Tasmanian law.

The relevant section of the *Criminal Code* is section 311, which says (relevantly):

'...charges of more than one crime may be joined in the same indictment, if those charges arise substantially out of the same facts or closely related facts, or are, or form part of, a series of crimes of the same or a similar character. In any other case an indictment shall charge one crime only...'

This section has been considered in many cases, including recently *Tasmania v Oakley* \[2015\] TASSC 12. In that case, Porter J considered the second limb of the section and said:

\[17\] *Under the second limb, not only do the charges need to be ones of crimes of the same or similar character, the charges must be or form part of a series of such crimes. To be, or be part of, a "series", there needs to be a nexus between the offence; a feature of similarity which enables them to be described as a series: Ludlow v Metropolitan Police Commissioner [1971] AC 29 at 38; R v Cranston [1986] 1 Qd R 159 at 164; R v Carr [2003] TASSC 123 at [6]. The section does not countenance the joinder of counts charging offences which are legally the same or similar in character but which in their factual setting are disparate. What is required is a sufficient correlation to enable the offences to be described as a "series" without straining the word beyond its reasonable meaning: De Jesus v The Queen (1986) 61 ALJR 1 per Dawson J at 9.*

### Prosecutorial Duties
[DOMAIN: Tasmanian Criminal Law] [ACT: N/A] [SECTION: N/A] [CITATION: N/A - Prosecutorial Duties] [CATEGORY: procedure] [TYPE: annotation] [TOPIC: Discussion on prosecutorial duties in Tasmanian law] [CONCEPTS: prosecutorial duties, accountability, justice, representation, ethics]

Practitioner commentary on the duties of a prosecutor within the criminal justice system.

> \[M\]ore than any other lawyer, \[the prosecutor\] must be especially careful to avoid misconduct because as a prosecutor well knows, her comments carry the imprimatur of the government ...  
>
> It is true prosecuting counsel ought not to press for a conviction. ... They should 'regard themselves' rather as 'ministers of justice' assisting in its administration than as advocates.  
>
> The United States Attorney is the representative not of an ordinary party to a controversy, but of a sovereignty whose obligation to govern impartially is as compelling as its obligation to govern at all, and whose interest, therefore, in a criminal prosecution is not that it shall win a case, but that justice shall be done. As such, he is in a peculiar and very definite sense the servant of the law, the twofold aim of which is that guilt shall not escape or innocence suffer. He may prosecute with earnestness and vigor -- indeed, he should do so. But, while he may strike hard blows, he is not at liberty to strike foul ones. It is as much his duty to refrain from improper methods calculated to produce a wrongful conviction as it is to use every legitimate means to bring about a just one.  
>
> It cannot be over-emphasized that the purpose of a criminal prosecution is not to obtain a conviction, it is to lay before a jury what the Crown considers to be credible evidence relevant to what is alleged to be a crime. Counsel have a duty to see that all available legal proof of the facts is presented: it should be done firmly and pressed to its legitimate strength but it must also be done fairly. The role of prosecutor excludes any notion of winning or losing ...  
>
> Prosecuting counsel in a criminal trial represents the State. The accused, the court and the community are entitled to expect that, in performing his function of presenting the case against an accused, he will act with fairness and detachment and always with the objectives of establishing the whole truth in accordance with the procedures and standards which the law requires to be observed and of helping to ensure that the accused's trial is a fair one.  
>
> Prosecutors are the most powerful players in the criminal justice system, capable of determining who should be charged and with what crimes. The duty to serve as a minister of justice is designed to limit abuse of this power and to compensate for the imbalance of resources that so often places the defense at a disadvantage. Demanding more of prosecutors than of other lawyers also fosters greater confidence in the legitimacy and accuracy of the criminal justice system.
