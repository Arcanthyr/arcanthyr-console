**SCHEDULE 2 - Custody and escort provisions**

**1.   Interpretation**

In this Schedule --

***custodian*** or *escort*, of a patient, means a police officer, MHO or authorised person who, by or under this Act, is empowered or authorised to take the patient into protective custody or under escort;

**1.   General powers, &c., of custodians and escorts**

To take a patient into protective custody or under escort --

\(a\) the custodian or escort may enlist the assistance of any person, including, if necessary, a police officer; and

\(b\) the custodian or escort (and any assistant) may use reasonable force against the patient if he or she resists being taken into protective custody or under escort; and

\(c\) the custodian or escort (and any assistant) may use reasonable force against anyone who may try to prevent the patient from being taken into protective custody or under escort; and

\(d\) the custodian or escort (if necessary with any assistant) may, without warrant and doing as little damage as possible, enter any premises if the custodian or escort reasonably believes that the patient may be found on those premises; and

\(e\) the custodian or escort (or any assistant) may take possession of and safeguard any medication, physical aid or other thing that the custodian or escort reasonably believes is or may be necessary to the patient\'s health, safety or welfare; and

\(f\) the custodian or escort (or any assistant) may take possession of and safeguard any medication, prescription or other thing that the custodian or escort reasonably believes is or may be relevant to the patient\'s examination, assessment, treatment or care; and

\(g\) the custodian or escort may, as circumstances require, transfer physical control of the patient to another custodian or escort or to another police officer, MHO or authorised person (who then has, and may exercise, any powers of the transferring custodian or escort under this clause); and

\(h\) the custodian or escort is not required to be in close physical proximity to the patient during any examination or assessment.

The prescribed offences appear in Schedule 2 to the *Justices Rules* 2003. It is always within the discretion of the justices as to whether or not they will hear a complaint ex parte (see *Justices Rules* 2003, rules 31, 32, and 34 (2)). If the justices in the exercise of their discretion determine to hear a complaint ex parte, then, in the circumstances postulated in the subsection, they are required to receive the complaint and summons in to evidence. It might be said that a summons, as distinct from a complaint, is unlikely to contain any relevant evidentiary material. This section cannot be used as proof of something not alleged in the complaint (*Howie v Gordon* (1986) 60 L.G.R.A. 36).

There is provision for the setting aside of ex parte convictions and orders in the *Justices Rules* 2003 (rule 38) and in the light of that, the cautionary dicta of Bray C.J in *Walker v Eves* (1976) 13 SASR 249 as to hearing complaints ex parte are of limited application in Tasmania.

The section presupposes that the complaint will be in proper form. The section only states the legal consequences which flow from the contents of a complaint in proper form (per Evatt J in *R v Hush; Ex parte Devanny* (1932) 48 CLR 487, at 513). If the allegations of fact in the complaint did not constitute the offence charged the subsection does not operate so as to enable the justices to convict (*Ex parte Ryan; Re Johnson* (1943) 44 SR (NSW) 12).

The term \"prima facie evidence\" probably means \"sufficient evidence\" (*Galvanized Iron Co v Westoby* (1852) 8 Ex 17), until rebutted (*Hewitt v Taylor* \[1896\] 1 QB 287; *Garbutt v Durham Joint Committee* \[1906\] AC 291; *Barraclough v Greenhough* \[1867\] LR 2 QB 612).

It is a matter for the Crown how it structures its case, what witnesses to call and the order of calling witnesses.

Public Interest - When determining whether or not to grant a restricted licence to a person a magistrate should take into account the following matters;

- Is it in the public interest to grant the applicant a restricted licence?

- Is the applicant likely to commit further offences whilst the holder of a restricted licence?

- The gravity of the offence for which the applicant was disqualified.

- The circumstances surrounding the commission of the offence.

- Will the applicant be likely to be source of danger on the road whilst driving pursuant to a restricted licence?
