*\[21\] In my view, the conduct of the defendant after being confronted by police does not amount to reasonable grounds for the requisite belief. He was entitled to refuse to allow police to search him, and entitled to walk away from them. The exercise of such rights is as consistent with innocence as with guilt, and could never form the basis of the belief necessary to carry out an arrest. A contrary conclusion would be entirely inconsistent with the rights which the statutory precondition to the exercise of the power of arrest is intended to protect. Further, the defendant\'s presence in the Mall close to the scene of the alleged crime could not have meant anything without understanding the factual basis of what had occurred. Once again, as pointed out by defence counsel, this information was as consistent with innocence as with guilt.*

*\[22\] The real issue in this case is whether it was reasonable for Sergeant Orr to uncritically accept the bare statement of the radio room operator that a person matching the defendant\'s description had committed a theft, as a basis for the requisite belief.*

*In Cotton v Ramm [\[1976\] 16 SASR 107](http://classic.austlii.edu.au/cgi-bin/LawCite?cit=%5b1976%5d%2016%20SASR%20107?stem=0&synonyms=0&query=poa1935140%20s39), Jacobs J dealing with a question of whether or not a police officer believed on reasonable grounds that a driver\'s ability to drive the motor vehicle was impaired, considered the question of whether the officer was entitled to rely upon information received from another officer as to the defendant\'s manner of driving. His Honour said this:*

*\"The least that the police officer concerned should do, before he forms a requisite belief, is to ascertain by enquiry as much as he can of the circumstances that might be relevant to his belief, if the information first supplied is not of itself sufficient, in terms of the statute, to justify the request for a breath test.\"*

*In Hibble v Phegan at \[52\], Zeeman J said:*

*\"A police officer\'s power of arrest has to be exercised by him or her personally, and on the facts known to him or her. The mere direction of a senior officer provides no lawful authority for the making of an arrest by the police officer to whom the direction is given. An arrest is a serious interference with personal liberty. That liberty may not be infringed except by lawful authority. The direction of a senior officer does not provide that lawful authority.\"*

*In this case, Sergeant Orr was not acting in response to a direction from a senior officer. However, she did accept uncritically what in effect amounted to a conclusion or mere allegation on the part of either the radio room operator or some other police officer, that the defendant had committed the theft. She did not inquire as to the circumstances, or the first-hand information upon which the report was based. It is instructive in this case to note that had she done so, she may have been told, that there was no evidence that a theft had taken place and that the conclusion that there had been a theft was based entirely on the unfounded suspicion of Mr Badcock.*

*In my view, the simple statement by the radio room operator that the theft had been committed was not sufficient to induce a state of belief in a reasonable person in the position of the police officer. There was no detail or explanation as to the information upon which this allegation was based. A reasonable person in Sergeant Orr\'s circumstances had no basis to exclude propositions consistent with innocence, for example that the report was based on the unfounded suspicion of another. Had Sergeant Orr been told that the defendant had been seen taking the items then she may well have had reasonable grounds to form the necessary belief. On the other hand, had she been told that the allegation arose merely from Mr Badcock\'s suspicion based on the appearance of the defendant, and his refusal to permit a search, then she would have realised that there was not a sufficient basis for an arrest. Without any explanation at all, the latter proposition could not be excluded.*

*Mr Fairley submitted that Sergeant Orr ought to have investigated the circumstances herself, by going to the shop and speaking to witnesses before carrying out the arrest. I do not believe that she was necessarily required to go to those lengths. There were good reasons for her to act quickly. Belief on reasonable grounds does not equate to proof. Further, the information available would not need to satisfy her of the guilt of the suspect beyond reasonable doubt. The assent of belief is given on more slender evidence than proof. Belief is an inclination of the mind towards assenting to, rather than rejecting, a proposition and the grounds which can reasonably induce that inclination of the mind may, depending on the circumstances, leave something to surmise or conjecture (See George v Rockett, supra). Had the radio room operator explained the circumstances upon which the allegation was based in a way which permitted a reasonable conclusion that the theft had been committed, then there may have been a sufficient basis for the arrest. However, in this case, Sergeant Orr had no information at all upon which to assess the cogency of the bald allegation, and accordingly did not have objectively reasonable grounds for her belief.*

NTB v DDB (A-4542-13T2) 9/9/15 (New Jersey Superior Court Appellate Division)

The original trial judge stated;

Under N.J.S.A. 2C:17-3(a)(1), a person is guilty of criminal mischief if he \"purposely or knowingly damages tangible property of ***another***\...\" The trial Judge reasoned that since \"the statute does not prohibit a person from causing damage to their ***own property***\", D.D.B. failed to show that N.T.B. committed an act against the tangible property of ***another***, thereby preluding a finding of criminal mischief.

The Appellate Court concluded that while \"each tenant by the entirety is a tenant in common with the other **during the joint lives of the spouses**\", \"each co-tenant has a **separate and distinct** freehold title and each holds his or her title and interest **independently of the others**.\" In light of this, the Appellate Division determined that N.T.B. and D.D.B. each held a separate and distinct interest in their home and therefore, N.T.B.\'s act of breaking down D.D.B\'s bedroom door did in fact destroy the \"property of another\" (due to D.D.B.\'s undivided interest in the home) and he therefore committed the predicate act of criminal mischief.

The Appellate Division opined that to conclude otherwise would \"permit a spouse to purposely and maliciously totally destroy his or her jointly owned marital home, without sanction, leaving no recourse for the innocent spouse to secure an FRO on the basis of the home\'s ruin.\"
