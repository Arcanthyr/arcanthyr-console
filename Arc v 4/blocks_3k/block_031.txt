**"Breach of the peace defined"**

As noted earlier, breach of the peace is not an offence, but instead it is the basis for the execution of the common law power which prevents public disorder. Furthermore, breach of the peace is one element of other public order offences such as unlawful assembly or riot.

In attempting to establish what breach of the peace entails, we can turn to the leading authority of the concept which can be found in *R v Howell*, where Watkins LJ in the English Court of Appeal, defined breach of the peace in the following way:

***"There is breach of the peace whenever harm is actually done or is likely to be done to a person or in his presence to his property or a person is in fear of being so harmed through an assault, an affray, a riot, unlawfully assembly or other disturbance."***

*R v Howell \[1981\] 3 All ER 383*

Cited in *Cintana v Burgoyne A Crim R 296 at para 13-19*

Broadly speaking, breach of the peace as defined in *Howell* has been generally accepted in Australian common law.

## No averment provision for interstate DVO

AFTER REVIEW (2 Sep 22), I now disagree with the below -- Section 17 of the DVO(NR) Act say that a non-local can be dealt with as a local. That means that a breach of non-local act is dealt with under s. 35(1) of the FVO act. Because it is now dealt with under s35(1), it enlivens s.35(3) -- the averment provision. Notice is still required, but dealt with as per below with a certificate from another state.

Section 35(3) of the* Family Violence Ac*t (Tas) says, among other things:

 

**\"*In any proceedings for an offence under s 35*** (which is what we charge interstate order breaches under), ***an allegation in the complaint is evidence that an FVO has been made against the person charged with an offence; that the order was in force at the time of the alleged contravention and that the condition alleged to have been breached formed part of the FVO.\"***

 

The point of this sub-section was to save us the hassle of having to prove a valid order in every complaint for a breach.

 

***\"FVO\"*** is specifically used in the sub-section.

 

***\"FVO\"*** is defined int the *Family Violence Ac*t (TAS) as \"***an FVO made under section 16 of the Family Violence Act (Tas)\"***

 

So, I don't think we can rely on this evidentiary provision in the *FV Act (Tas)* because this is a QLD DVO\...

 

Recourse then to the *Domestic Violence Orders (National Recognition*) Act\...

 

The relevant sections are ss 4, 10, 15, 16, 17 and 31 of the DVO Act

* *

**Section 4:** Interpretation section  

 

**Section 10(b):** Recognises interstate DVOs made in participating Jurisdiction (QLD is one such jurisdiction)

 

**Section 15(3):** Says that a recognised DVO is enforceable when the defendant is ***properly notified*** of the making of the DVO under the law of the jurisdiction it was made (QLD)

 

**Section 16(2):** Defines "***properly notified***" in the context of interstate DVOs. Basically it requires compliance with the requirements of the State's legislation under which the order was made (QLD). In Tas it would have to be served or made in the defendant\'s presence. I imagine most other States are the same. 

 

**Section 17(1):** Says that where there is a breach of a recognised, enforceable, DVO, it can be enforced as if it were a breach of an FVO under the *FV Act* (Tas).

 

**Section 31(1):** Evidentiary provision as to "***proper notification***". Basically, it says that an authorised officer under another jurisdiction\'s legislation can produce a certificate to the effect that the making of a DVO has been ***properly notified*** under the laws of that jurisdiction. The certificate is evidence of the matter contained in it.

 

**Section 31(4):** says that if the certificate is signed by a person purporting to be an authorised officer that. in itself, is evidence that they were an authorised officer.

 

**Section 31(5):** defined authorised officer in another jurisdiction as being "...a person (whether or not designated as an authorised officer) who is authorised under the law of another jurisdiction to issue a certificate certifying that the making or variation of a DVO has been properly notified under the law of that jurisdiction"

 

So, to prove ***proper notification*** of an interstate DVO we need to identify that jurisdictions authorised officers, and get a certificate from them.

 

BUT, even if we do that, the *DVO Act* only provides an evidentiary provision to the effect of proving that the interstate DVO was ***properly notified***.

 

WHAT WE ARE STILL MISSING, is an equivalent to section 35(3) of our *FV Act*. That is, an evidentiary provision as to the validity, conditions, and force of the DVO at the time of offending. 

My reading is that we would need admissible evidence from the court of the specific jurisdiction to prove this, and that we can\'t simply rely on the averment in the complaint, as we do with local orders.

# Driving:

***Cooley v Lowe***

For it to be proven that person was 'driving' a motor vehicle the following matters are to be considered,

- The degree to which the 'driver' was operating the controls of the motor vehicle (steering, brakes etc.)

- The distance travelled.

- The road conditions, (undulations, corners etc.)

- The speed of the vehicle

- If the vehicle was being towed (as in this case), the length of the tow rope, communication (if any) between the vehicles, the manner in which the towed vehicle was steered and stopped

*... "driving"*, requires proof that the person charged *"\...had the capacity to do so, and that there was at least a reasonable possibility, if *not* likelihood, that he would attempt to drive the vehicle and so endanger the public\..." *in accordance with the Full Court decision in *Cooley v Lowe *\[1984\] Tas R 107.[^\[2^](http://www.austlii.edu.au/cgi-bin/viewdoc/au/cases/tas/TASMC/2020/17.html#fn2)

\[27\] In my view, when parliament amended the extended definition of *"driving"* by introducing s 2(4) and s 2(5), it did not intend to overturn the effect of the Full Court's decision in *Cooley v Lowe*. Indeed the amended definition is entirely consistent with the approach taken in that case.* *This view is reinforced by the nature of the Full Court's reasoning in that case where the Court approved what was said by Morris CJ in *Smith v* *Westell.*[^\[14\]^](http://www.austlii.edu.au/cgi-bin/viewdoc/au/cases/tas/TASMC/2020/17.html#fn14) In the relevant passages, the Court emphasised that the words extending the meaning of *"driving"* still require proof of a link between;

a.  The acts or omissions contemplated by the definition provisions; and

b.  The possibility or likelihood that the person will move the vehicle.
