### Abandonment

Property that has been abandoned can not be stolen (see *post ss* 18 -- 24). If property has not been abandoned, but the defendant believes that it has, under the old law he could not be convicted of theft, whether his belief was reasonable or unreasonable : see *R v White* (1912) 7 Cr App. R 266; *Ellerman's Wilson Line Ltd. V Webster* \[1952\] 1 Ll.L.R. 179, D.C. It is submitted that this is still the law. Whether or not a mistake of fact has to be reasonable (see ss 17-4 *et seq.*), there would be no evidence that the defendant was acting dishonestly.

#### Must take reasonable steps to find owner

***Maingay v Charles \[2009\] TASSC 98***

"The respondent\'s stated belief was in effect that he did not believe he could discover the owner by any reasonable means. While there is no stated requirement in s226 that the belief itself be a reasonable one, it would make a nonsense of the legislation were an alleged offender to simply be able to state that he did not hold the requisite belief, notwithstanding that that stated belief might be entirely inconsistent with the surrounding circumstances, and for that statement to be determinative. Of course, the belief referred to in s226 is one that an owner can be discovered by \"reasonable means\". (see below)"

Maingay also sites Alexander Gordon MacDonald \[1983\] 8 A Crim R 248

(The defendant found foreign currency and kept it, and made no attempt to locate an owner and claimed he believed it was abandoned)

In Alexander Gordon MacDonald \[1983\] 8 A Crim R 248, the Court of Criminal Appeal of New South Wales considered how the state of mind of a finder of property is to be established when the finder has volunteered no information pointing to their state of mind at the relevant time. The Court (Lee, Maxwell and Yeldham JJ) observed (at p251):

\"The guilty mind or animus furandi requisite in larceny by finding is thus the belief of the finder that the owner can be found, and unless that belief is established beyond reasonable doubt, a conviction for larceny is not open. The Crown of course carries the onus or proving that belief beyond reasonable doubt, and there is no onus on the accused in any circumstances to prove that he did not have such a belief or that he believed the goods had been abandoned, or that the owner could not be found (May v O\'Sullivan \[1955\] HCA 38; (1955) 92 C.L.R. 654). How then is the requisite belief in the finder to be established when the finder has volunteered no information pointing to the state of his mind at the relevant time, as is the case here? In Russell on Crime

(12 th edition) the learned author, at p1014, states:

"In cases of taking on finding some of the strongest circumstances to rebut the implication that such taking was felonious \...are those which show that \[the taker\] endeavoured to discover the true owner and kept the goods till it might reasonably be supposed that the true owner could not be found. The finders belief, in our view, is to be inferred from the facts and circumstances surrounding the finding and the taking of the goods, and in this respect regard may be had not only to what the finder does in relation to the goods but also what he does not do that might reasonably be regarded as consistent with the actions of an honest man finding goods. Did the finder examine it closely to see if it gave any clue to its owner by name tag or otherwise? What avenues were reasonably open to the finder to locate the owner? A person finding goods may be taken to know that the person who has lost those goods may well retrace his steps with a view to recovering them, and so the leaving of the finders name and address with someone at the place of discovery may be one means of locating the

owner. It is common knowledge in our community, and the finder would know, that the police will receive lost property handed in and take care of it and accordingly the finder would think that the loser might make an enquiry of the police in the area where he believes he may have lost his property. It is common knowledge that newspapers publish ,,lost and found columns. These and perhaps other considerations would ordinarily be present to the mind of an honest person finding property in a suburb of Sydney. Each case nmust be looked at according to its own facts, and the place where the property is found and the nature of the property will, in most instances, readily indicate the avenues that are reasonably open to

find the owner.\"

### Difference between requirements under s 37G and 37GA:

***Baker v Smith* \[2025\] TASSC 43**

**Facts**

- Baker's vehicle crashed (Wynyard, 18 Mar 2023). Driver left scene.

- Police served Baker (registered owner) with a **driver demand notice** (s 37GA *Police Offences Act 1935*), requiring details of driver/occupant.

- Baker did not comply.

- Magistrate convicted him (Aug 2024). Baker sought review.

------------------------------------------------------------------------

**Issues**

1.  Does s 37G require only an **allegation** of an offence, or officer's **satisfaction** of it?

2.  Was there sufficient proof Baker was the **registered owner**?

3.  Was the notice **invalid/ambiguous** (re: offence details; "driver or occupant" wording)?

4.  Did the officer lack **reasonable belief** an offence occurred?

5.  Did issuing the notice unlawfully abrogate Baker's **right to silence/self-incrimination** (given officer was "99.99% sure" Baker was driver)?

------------------------------------------------------------------------

**Decision**

- **Appeal dismissed** -- conviction upheld.

------------------------------------------------------------------------

**Ratio**

- **s 37G precondition:** only requires an **allegation**, not proof or officer's satisfaction.

- **Separate offences:** s 37G (demand) and s 37GA (notice) each create distinct offences with different penalties.

- **Ownership proof:** admissions and conduct can suffice; certificate of registration not essential.

- **Notice validity:**

  - "Indicate" = general statement of offence, not exact section.

  - Context showed allegation was against **driver**, not occupant → no ambiguity.

- **Reasonable belief:** Magistrate applied wrong test (belief vs allegation), but no miscarriage of justice.

- **Right to silence:** s 37G clearly abrogates it; no implied "necessity" limit.

------------------------------------------------------------------------

**Significance**

- Confirms **low threshold** for valid driver demand: mere allegation is enough.

- Clarifies ownership can be proved by **conduct/admissions**.

- Confirms strict but clear statutory abrogation of silence/self-incrimination rights.

- On review, findings will stand if **reasonably open**.

**Case:** *Baker v Smith* \[2025\] TASSC 43

  ----------------------------------------------- ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------ ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------
  **1. Preconditions (s 37G vs s 37GA)**          Proof required that officer was *satisfied* an offence occurred (s 37GA(1)), not just an allegation.                     Wrong -- s 37G only requires an *allegation* of an offence. Satisfaction standard in s 37GA not imported. Conviction can rest on allegation alone.

  **2. Ownership**                                No proper evidence Baker was registered owner. Body worn footage not enough; should have had registration certificate. ### "Possession" circumstantial evidence only.

**Be careful as to assumptions of possession**

**"recent possession doctrine" does not exist -- it is circumstantial evidence.**

**Roughan v Haines \[2019\] TASMC 7 (25 November 2019)**

*\[17\] Elements (b), (c), (d) and (e) are all circumstantial evidence pointing towards guilt. The so called "doctrine of recent possession" which, if established, presumed guilt and reversed the onus of proof has long been recognised as having no place in modern criminal law: see R v Wanganeen [\[1988\] 50 SASR 433](http://www.austlii.edu.au/cgi-bin/LawCite?cit=%5b1988%5d%2050%20SASR%20433) at 34 to 42; see also Wallis v Tasmania [\[2007\] TASSC 4](http://www.austlii.edu.au/cgi-bin/viewdoc/au/cases/tas/TASSC/2007/4.html) per Evans J at paragraphs 78 to 80. Therefore, if there is evidence -- as here -- that a defendant was in possession of a lot of property stolen from Begents that is evidence which falls to be considered as circumstantial evidence.*
