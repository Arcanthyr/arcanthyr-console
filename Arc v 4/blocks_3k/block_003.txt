**Search and seizure (Search Warrant Question Template)**

**Valid entry to premises for purpose of search and Seizure**

Common law protection of property is extensive. Fundamental right to private property -- and to exclude others from it -- see for example HCA in *Coco v R*.

According to *Kuru v NSW*, the police have no special powers to gain lawful entry to premises beyond authorisation in the common law or statute. To lawfully enter premises they will require either:

- Consent; or

- A licence or lawful authorisation.

Further, the common law authorises the police to enter premises;

- to prevent a breach of the peace; or

- Make a lawful arrest and then enter and seize items in the possession of the arrestee.

To otherwise gain lawful entry; the police require a ***search warrant***.

**Search warrants**

**Application**

An application for a search warrant should be made in writing by the applicant (but this is not required by the *Search Warrants Act* in Tasmania). It must then be considered by a judicial officer (usually Justice of the Peace or Magistrate).

The general provision most commonly relied upon for an application for a search warrant is contained in s5(1) of the Search Warrants Act:

**5. When search warrants can be issued**

**(1)** An issuing officer may issue a warrant to search premises if the officer is satisfied by information on oath that ***there are reasonable grounds for suspecting that there is, or there will be within the next 72 hours, any evidential material at the premises***.

But, the *Search Warrants* *Act* does not limit or exclude the operation of any other law purporting to grant powers of search, seizure, arrest or detention (s4).

***Duties of issuing officer***

- A warrant can only be issued if the conditions in s5 (above) are satisfied; or the conditions imposed in any other enabling legislation being relied upon.

- Usually require ***reasonable grounds for suspecting*** that the property will be on the premises.

- The nature of the search warrant process is to ***interpose a judicial officer*** between the executive and citizens to act as a control on excessive executive action; the judicial officer acts as a 'fundamental safeguard': *George v Rocket*.

- To make this process effective; the judicial officer is therefore required to ***bring an independent mind to bear on the process***: *George v Rocket*.

- The ***judicial officer*** must therefore ***be satisfied*** that there are ***grounds for issuing the warrant***:

- *MacLeod* and *George v Rocket* confirm that this requires :

  - there be ***objectively reasonable grounds*** for the belief.

  - I.e. was the information ***sufficient to induce the requisite suspicion*** in a ***reasonable person***?

- A suspicion is more than a mere idle wondering (*George v Rocket)*.

- It is clear that the judicial officer cannot simply 'rubber stamp' the application by simply relying on the suspicions of the applicant: *MacLeod*.

- So importantly, it is the ***state of mind of the issuing officer*** which will be the Court's focus: *MacLeod*.

- However, judicial officer is not required to go 'behind the application' to investigate further or seek more information unless there are clear ambiguities or other questions raised by the application: *Beneficial Finance Corp v AFP* and *Jackson v Mijovich*.

***Duties of applicant -- extent of disclosure required***

- Competing authority on this point; argue both ways.

- Compare *Karina Fisheries* and *Lego Australia*.

- The applicant for a warrant should disclose all matters that would affect the issuing of the warrant otherwise it may be invalid: *Karina Fisheries.*

- The search warrant will only be invalid for non-disclosure where there has been some fraud, half-truth or misrepresentation on the part of the applicant: *Lego Australia.*

- But practically, it is good practice to disclose all information.

***Particularity in warrant***

There is a need for sufficient particularity in the application; state the place, name of applicant etc. Also need to describe the nature of the offence so that it can be established what the search is for and to establish boundaries for the search. Some minor errors will be fine though provided the nature of the offence is clear: *NSW v Corbet*.

Come to preliminary conclusion on whether warrant would be struck out as a result of the application.

**Execution of the warrant**

***Has a proper Announcement been made?***

The requirements to make a 'proper announcement' as contained in the arrest cases of *Lippl* and *O'Neil* have been embodied in s19 of the *Search Warrants Act*. As a result;

(1) The police must announce that they are authorised to enter the premises, and give opportunity for person to allow entry.

However, not required to give such an announcement if the police believe on reasonable grounds (see cases in relation to arrest *Rockett*) that immediate entry is required to ensure the safety of any person or the effective execution of the warrant (for instance if they believe evidence is about to be destroyed).

Also note, s8 of the *Search Warrants Act*; requirement to show particulars of the warrant to the occupier of the premises (if present).

***Scope of the search warrant (i.e. seizing items not particularised in the warrant).***

It is clear that the police cannot use a warrant to go on a fishing exercise: *Crowley v Murphy*.

However, whether they can lawfully seize items not mentioned in the warrant will depend on whether the conditions from *Ghani v Jones*, which are now embodied (mostly) in s6 of the *Search Warrants Act*, can be satisfied:

(1) Must relate to a serious offence;

(2) Property must be the fruity of the crime or the means by which it was committed, or material evidence of its commission; and

(3) That the person in possession was involved in the commission of the crime either as a principal or accessory.

In addition:

(4) Police must keep them for no longer that necessary to complete investigations or to preserve as evidence; and

(5) The lawfulness of the police conduct must be judged at the time of the seizure, not by what happens later (hindsight or subsequent events cannot justify the seizure).

Under s6(e) of the *Search Warrants Act* the executing officer can seize any material found on the premises that they believe on reasonable grounds to be either:

- Evidential material in relation to the offence which the warrant relates; or

- Evidential material in relation to another offence.

***Application to legal professional privilege***

Legal professional privilege is a fundamental common law right; therefore search warrants to not apply to documents covered by LPP (see *Baker v Campbell* and s22 *Search Warrants Act*). To override this; must be made ***very clear*** by legislation.

NB practical approach AFP and Law Reform Commission developed; documents bundled up and given to third party to determine if they are, in fact, privileged. Person alleging privileged has four days to establish privileged status. But see *Oke v Commr. AFP* for practical illustration of the difficulties with this process.

**Conclusion**

If warrant is invalid, then the entry is unlawful and all evidence obtained will be at the judge's discretion to exclude it on the basis it was improperly or illegally obtained evidence (s138 of *Evidence Act*).

**1. Right to Silence**

Art 14(3)(g) ICCPR

- right of a person not to be compelled to testify himself or herself or to confess guilt

- [One rationale:]{.underline} it acts as a curb upon the excesses of law enforcement agencies

- Thought to derive in CL from the excesses of the Star Chamber in UK which had the power to 'press' (torture) confessions out of people

- Linked to presumption of innocence and onus of proof BRD on the Crown

- [Scope:]{.underline} Considered in R v Director of Serious Fraud Office, ex parte Smith \[1993\] AC 1 at 30 by Lord Mustal: Right to silence does not cover a single right, rather it consists of a 'bundle of rights' which have a number of different historical sources and motivations:

- 1\. General immunity of everyone to be free from being compelled to answer questions on pain of punishment

- 2\. General immunity of everyone from being compelled to answer question on pain of punishment *that may incriminate them*.

- 3\. Specific immunity possessed by suspects --when being interviewed by law enforcement officials to not answer questions

- 4\. General immunity from being questioned at all when charged with a criminal offence (i.e. once charged, agents should not question them further at all)

- 5\. Trial immunity: from being compelled to answer on pain of punishment at trial

- 6\. Trial immunity for accused: from having adverse inferences drawn against them from refusing to answer questions on trial

- Based on the notion that we all have a right to tell other people to 'mind their own business'

- These 6 instances of Right to Silence are very rigid: only allow for circumstances of oral evidence, thus may not extend to other types of evidence (such as ID parade, photos etc)

> [Other immunities derived from people subject to investigation:]{.underline}

- Derive from CL dislike of the behavior of the Star Chamber (and 'pressing' confessions)

- Adherence to the idea that any evidence obtained by pressure is inherently unreliable

- Expanded to include notions of fair play and a fair trial

- And also desire to minimize risk of unfair trial

- Most require a 'reasonable grounds for belief' that a search is necessary. Some terminology is 'reasonable suspicion'.

*Lindley v Rutter* \[1981\] QB 128

- 1\) Must be on reasonable grounds to conduct a personal search

- 2\) PO can only take items from you that relate to an offence OR narrower, search for items in relation only to the offence that you have been arrested for

- 3\) PO can take items that may assist in escaping or may injure the arrestee or arrestor

**Search of Premises**

GR: an occupier may deny access to anyone. There must clear legislative authority to allow someone to override that.

CL Starting point: **(narrow)**

search of premises only permitted when police enter on to premises pursuant to an arrest, or with the consent of owner/occupier, pursuant to a search warrant. Originally at CL, SW only permitted in order to retrieve stolen property.

Extent of search permissible at CL: Dillon and O'Brien

At Genreally extends power s to search premises pursuant to a warrant or pre or post arrest.

*Coco v R* (1994) 179 CLR 427 at pp 435-6 (see also *New South Wales v Ibbett* (2006) 229 CLR 638 *and Kuru v New South Wales* (2008) 246 ALR 260 at \[40\]-\[55\])

No clear unambiguous authorization for police to go on to private premises and install a listening device, therefore the legislation did not allow them to do so.

*Ibbett*: if police do trespass onto private premises, aggravated damages are unacceptable.

*Dillon v O'Brien & Davies* (1887) 16 Cox CC 245 -- warrant for arrest

Where police enter premises to arrest, can search an arrestee and the area immediately around the arrestee (e.g. table/desk) for the same purpose (to find evidence of the offence, secure any items that may be used to effect an escape or cause danger to themselves or someone else)

*Jeffrey v Black* \[1977\] 3 WLR 895

F: police arrest accussed outside own premises, can they then take the accussed to his home to search?

H: That search could not be considered to be a search 'incidental' to an arrest (they should have obtained a search warrant)

So...cant arrest in one location and then take to their home to search it (not considered a search incidental to an arrest)

*Ghani v Jones* \[1970\] 1 QB 693

Vastly increased police power of search from *Dillon v O'Brien* when effecting an arrest or under a search warrant. Why? Probs, parliament was being very slow to expand police investigative tools in this area. Very different to that in Australia (which has always had quite extensive search warrants and search provisions).

Court of Appeal: If the police enter premises lawfully in order to affect an arrest or pursuant to a search warrant, they may search the premises to find evidentiary material in relation to the offence that they are arresting for/search warrant for. If during the course of the search find material about another offence, they may cease those items as well, provided they behave reasonably and don't retain the items ceased for an unreasonable period of time. (confirmed in *Rottman)*

**CL Re: searching a premises when police have entered NOT pursuant to an arrest or search warrant**

In *Ghani* the PO had entered with the consent of the occupier. H: they could search in this circumstances, not only to the offence they were investigating, but any other items they reasonably believed to afford evidence to another arrest. But court imposed 5 pre-conditions on the search and procedure:

1.  RB it is a serious offence evidence

2.  Property was the fruit of the crime , or the means of its commission, or material evidence of its commission

3.  That the person in possession was involved in the crimes as a principle or accessory

4.  The police must keep it for no longer than necessary

5.  The lawfulness of the police conduct is to be judged at the time they conducted the search (not with the value of hindsight)

**Declined *Ghani v Jones***

*Challenge Plastics Pty Ltd v Collector of Customs* (1993) 115 ALR 149 per Heerey J at 157-157

Good analysis of the CL (tried to argue the dissent in *Rottman*)

Heery J: police search did not comply with the statutory provision (went beyond this). Then argued that *Ghani* applied to extend powers beyond the statute.

Re Ghani: No search warrant, arrest warrant, arrest, did not involve stolen goods, the extention of the norm made by Ghani is perhaps even more suprising given denning in some other case. The requirement of a warrant is a statutory safeguard to avoid arbitrary interference with personal property. The State wishing to cease property must swear on oath of the need to cease it (protective safeguard between investigators and citizens). Requires a degree of specificity --and courts will scrutinize warrants very carefully to ensure warrants have been complied with. *Ghani* really makes the process of obtaining a search warrant pretty irrelevant, and will no longer apply the safeguards they were designed to do. If *Ghani* is right, state officials are better off without a warrant at all.
