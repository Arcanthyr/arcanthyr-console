# Mistake of Law: Definition and Case Law
[DOMAIN: Tasmanian Criminal Law] [CATEGORY: doctrine] [TYPE: doctrine] [TOPIC: mistake of law does not exculpate liability] [CONCEPTS: mistake of law, mistake of fact, legal advice, criminal liability, court decisions] [CITATION: hoc-b017-m001-mistake-of-law] [ACT: None] [CASE: Thomas v R, Strathfield Municipal Council v Elvy, Minear v Rudrum, Brooks v Roberts, Wroblewski v Starling, Ostrowski v Palmer]

Commonly known as the defence of ignorance and is not a ground for exculpation. It involves a misconception that occurs when a person with complete knowledge of the facts reaches an erroneous conclusion as to their legal effect; an incorrect opinion or inference, arising from a flawed evaluation of the facts.

A mistake of law, as distinct from a mistake of fact, does not generally exculpate the defendant's criminal liability.

Determining what is a mistake of fact and what is a mistake of law is not always straightforward. The process is further complicated when the mistake involves a combination of factual and legal elements. In *Thomas v R* (1937) 59 CLR 279, Dixon J (Latham CJ and Rich J agreeing) drawing on English authorities it concluded that such mixed mistakes should be considered mistakes of fact. However, more recently in *Strathfield Municipal Council v Elvy* (1992) NSWLR 745 at 751, Gleeson CJ held that mixed mistakes will not ordinarily constitute mistakes of fact.

In determining this issue the court may be assisted by *Minear v Rudrum* \[2001\] WASCA 10 where the Court of Appeal dismissed an appeal because the mistake was legal not factual.

In that case the appellant was the holder of a provisional driver licence and issued with a traffic infringement notice resulting in automatic disqualification. The appellant's mother managed the infringement notice on behalf of her son and made enquiries with the traffic infringement section. Here erroneous advice was received to the effect that disqualification would not take effect until the licence was collected.

The appellant relied on such advice and was charged and convicted of driving offences.

On appeal it was held that the cancellation of the licence arose automatically by operation of the law and therefore any mistake he made was one of law. It was accepted that the appellant was advised that his licence remained valid and the honest and reasonable limbs of his claim were (presumably) made out, but dismissed.

The advice he received was advice as to the law and provision of the law remained applicable irrespective of such advice.

*Brooks v Roberts*, unreported, SCt of WA, Library 970072, 17 February 1997 was followed. This involved circumstances where the court failed to impose the minimum statutory period of disqualification and a correction of sentence application was made. The application was not served on the defendant and resulted in the sentence being corrected ex parte. The defendant then drove contrary to the disqualification and it was held his belief as to the validity of his licence could have been based only on his ignorance of the law.

A similar case of *Wroblewski v Starling* \[1987\] WAR 233 was distinguished as that involved particular administrative action that had to be taken to effect the period of driver suspension.

It is submitted that in the present circumstances there was no further action required to be taken to effect the court sanctioned disqualification period. It took effect immediately and any subsequent erroneous action taken by the Registrar of Motor Vehicle, or the advantage the defendant took of that mistake, does not impact upon the validity of the sentence.

A leading case which emphasises the distinction between mistake of law and mistake of fact is the High Court decision of *Ostrowski v Palmer* \[2004\] HCA 30.

The appellant was a fisherman who had sought advice from the Western Australia Fisheries Department as to regulations applicable to rock lobster fishing. He was provided with insufficient information of which he relied upon and was found in breach of an area restriction.

The High Court found the mistake to be one of law and not fact. In particular, Hugh J at paragraph 53 held "it is irrelevant that Mr Palmer's mistake was induced by the conduct of an employee of Fisheries WA. That conduct cannot convert a mistake as to the applicable law into a mistake of fact. If a defendant knows all the relevant facts that constitute the offence and acts on erroneous advice as to the legal effect of those facts, the defendant, like the adviser, has been mistaken as to the law, not the facts".

# Honest and Reasonable Mistake of Fact: General Principles
[DOMAIN: Tasmanian Criminal Law] [CATEGORY: doctrine] [TYPE: doctrine] [TOPIC: honest and reasonable mistake of fact test] [CONCEPTS: honest mistake, reasonable belief, evidentiary burden, criminal responsibility, cross-examination] [CITATION: hoc-b017-m002-honest-reasonable-mistake] [ACT: Road Safety (Alcohol and Drugs) Act 1970] [CASE: Hindrum v Lane, Pearce v Stanton]

It is worth noting as a preliminary issue that in *Hindrum v Lane* \[2014\] Tennant J referred with approval to the comment of Rowland J in the Western Australian case of *Pearce v Stanton* \[1984\] WAR 358 (at 363) to the effect that a consideration of whether in the circumstances a belief is reasonable must also look at the policy behind the relevant statute. The section of the *Road Safety (Alcohol and Drugs) Act 1970* which is the subject of the charge was introduced in 2005 by an amending Act. During the Second Reading Speech introducing the amending Bill, the Minister responsible noted 'the purpose of this Bill is to reform the Act to address the road safety issues associated with people who drive motor vehicles with illicit drugs in their blood, increasing the risk of road trauma on Tasmanian roads.' It is submitted this purpose should be kept in mind by a Court considering any question of honest and reasonable mistake in relation to the presence of amphetamine in a driver’s system.

The defence of honest and reasonable mistake is raised by the evidence adduced by the defendant. The defendant has no special burden when raising the defence of honest and reasonable mistake; they need only raise the evidentiary burden to satisfy its foundation. This can occur in the cross-examination of prosecution witnesses, though.

Notwithstanding common usage, it is strictly not a defence. Rather, if held, it means the prosecution has been unable to establish all of the essential ingredients of the charge.

Once raised by the defendant, the onus is on the prosecuting authority to dispel any doubt that may have been raised by the evidence that the acts performed were a result of honest and reasonable mistake. The standard is beyond reasonable doubt.

The general test is whether the defendant had an honest and reasonable mistaken belief in a certain state of things which, if that state of things existed, the defendant's actions would have been innocent.

The belief is based on both a subjective and objective test.

The court in *Hindrum v Lane* \[2014\] TASFC 5 made the point that the requirement that the belief be reasonable is a requirement that the belief honestly held by the accused person is a belief held on reasonable grounds, not that the belief itself must be objectively reasonable.

Honest (subjective)

Reasonable (mixed -- subjective attributes of the specific person in subjective context against an objective standard)

When raised, the prosecution must prove beyond reasonable doubt that the applicant did not act pursuant to an honest belief, held on reasonable grounds, in a state of facts which, if true, would render him not guilty of the offence.

The first question to be asked is whether Mr King’s assertions are sufficient to raise the issue of honest and reasonable mistake. It is submitted this is arguable, given Mr King simply made statements that could not be regarded as evidence, in the sense of admissible evidence, and those statements were untested by cross-examination and were unsupported by any other evidence. Furthermore, there was no opportunity for prosecution to test other aspects of Mr King's statements that might lead to questions about his credibility.

For example, at one stage in his statement, he referred to unknown persons at the birthday party calling Police. He also referred to his previous assistance to Police on an unidentified matter. Given there were no particulars given about either of those statements, prosecution has been unable to test their veracity.

As then Acting Chief Magistrate Daly said in the analogous case of *Barnes v Fowler* \[2016\] TASMC 8, referring to drinks being spiked, '...in order for the evidence on this issue to be sufficient there would need to be something more than the merest of possibilities of an opportunity for such an occurrence'.

### Duress Under Criminal Code Act 1924
[DOMAIN: Tasmanian Criminal Law] [ACT: Criminal Code Act 1924] [SECTION: 20] [CITATION: Duress Definition and Elements] [CATEGORY: legal doctrine] [TYPE: legal doctrine] [TOPIC: Legal framework for duress as a defense] [CONCEPTS: duress, threat, criminal code, immediate harm, defense criteria]

**Essential Elements:** (***Hurley and Murray \[1967\] VR 129)***

1) There must be a threat of immediate death or grievous bodily harm.
2) The accused must be subject TO the threats.
3) The person making the threat must be actually present at the commission of the offence.
4) The accused must believe that the threat will be executed.
5) The offence committed by the accused must not be an 'excepted offence'.
6) The defence will not be available to the accused if he is a party to any association or conspiracy that rendered him subject to compulsion.

# Express and implied threats
[DOMAIN: Tasmanian Criminal Law] [CATEGORY: doctrine] [TYPE: doctrine] [TOPIC: threat must conform with effective duress requirements] [CONCEPTS: duress, implied threats, harm, criminal act, necessity] [CITATION: hoc-b018-m001-express-implied-threats] [ACT: None] [CASE: The King v Anna Rowan]

The necessity for the accused to be subject to a threat of infliction of the relevant form of harm if the accused fails to commit the acts that constitute the offence charged has been a consistent feature of the consideration of the common law of duress by intermediate courts of appeal in the common law jurisdictions in this country.

The nature of the requisite threat is illustrated by the facts of *R v Dawson* and *R v Lorenz*. In *Dawson*, the accused escaped from prison but led evidence that he did so because he feared for his life after receiving threats from other prisoners. The Full Court held that duress was not open because the threats were not made on the basis that, if he did not escape, he would be subject to violence. In *Lorenz*, the accused robbed a supermarket employee with a knife after her de facto partner threatened to kill her if she did not obtain enough money to re-register his car. The accused was pregnant at the time. After noting *Hurley* and *Dawson*, Crispin J held that the defence of duress was not available as the accused's de facto partner did not direct her to commit the robbery.

However, neither *Dawson* nor *Lorenz* suggests that a threat to inflict the relevant form of harm if the accused fails to commit the acts constituting the charge cannot be implied. In *Hurley*, Smith J postulated the scenario of armed men taking possession of a house at gunpoint and sending the accused to commit a crime on their behalf while retaining a hostage such that "the threat to the hostage's life, *whether it is formulated in words or left unsaid*, may be held to be, during the execution of the commission, sufficiently present and continuing, imminent and impending to found the defence of duress" (emphasis added).

The references in *Hurley* to the threat being "present and continuing, imminent and impending" do not require that all the conduct relied upon as giving rise to the threat be engaged in immediately prior to the crime being committed. Instead, those statements should be taken as conveying a requirement that the threat conform with the description of the equivalent aspect of duress noted in other cases, namely that the threat be "operative" or "effective" at the time the accused committed the acts that constitute the offence charged. The relevant threat does not have to immediately precede the accused's criminal act if there was no reasonable opportunity to avoid committing the offence in the meantime (that being the issue raised by element (viii) in *Hurley*).

# Duty to declare basis of alleged criminal liability
[DOMAIN: Tasmanian Criminal Law] [CATEGORY: doctrine] [TYPE: doctrine] [TOPIC: obligation of Crown to declare basis of liability] [CONCEPTS: criminal liability, Crown Prosecutor, principal, accessory, evidence] [CITATION: hoc-b018-m002-duty-to-declare-liability] [ACT: Criminal Code 1924] [CASE: R v Tangye]

The obligation of the Crown Prosecutor in opening the Crown case is not merely to outline the facts which the Crown proposes to establish in evidence. It is also to indicate, in conceptual terms, the nature of the Crown case. This is to assist both the trial judge and counsel for the accused, more so than the jury. It is essential that any doubt about the nature of the Crown case, conceptually, be removed at that early stage. If it is not done at that stage, or if there had been some change in its nature since the case was opened, it is vital that it be identified with some precision, in the absence of the jury, before counsel commence their final addresses. It becomes very difficult for the judge sensibly to make alterations to directions already given once it is learnt that the issues are different to those which had been assumed to exist.

A person who contributes to the commission of a crime/offence may in certain circumstances be charged as a principal to that crime/offence - if it can be shown that the contribution was done or made with knowledge, willingness and a desire to assist. If the offence constitutes a crime then such a person is charged pursuant to section **3 of the Criminal Code, 1924**:

**3. Which parties to crimes to be deemed principals in the first degree**

(1) Where a crime is committed, each of the following persons is deemed to be a party to, and to be guilty of, the crime, and may be charged with actually committing it:

(a) every person who actually commits the crime;

(b) every person who does any act or makes any omission for the purpose of enabling or aiding another person to commit the crime;

(c) every person who abets another person in committing the crime;

(d) every person who instigates any other person to commit the crime.

(2) Any person who instigates another to do any act or make any omission of such a nature that, if he had himself done the act or made the omission, the act or omission would have constituted a crime on his part, is guilty of the same crime as if he had himself done the act or made the omission; and may be charged with himself committing that crime.

If the offence involves a simple offence then such person is charged pursuant to section **73 of the Justices Act, 1959**:

**73. Accessories**

(1) Subject to any contrary intention in the Act creating the offence, where a simple offence is committed, each of the following persons is deemed to be a party to, and to be guilty of, the offence, and may be charged with actually committing it, namely:

(a) a person who actually commits the offence;

(b) a person who does any act or makes any omission for the purpose of enabling or aiding another person to commit the offence;

(c) a person who abets another person in committing the offence;

(d) a person who instigates another person to commit the offence.

(2) A person who instigates another person to do an act or make an omission of such a nature that, if he had himself done the act or made the omission, the act or omission would have constituted a simple offence on his part, is guilty of the same offence as if he had himself done the act or made the omission, and may be charged with himself committing the offence.

(3) A person who is alleged to have instigated, aided, or abetted the commission of a simple offence may be convicted upon a complaint charging him with having committed the offence, or upon a complaint charging him with having instigated, aided, or abetted, as the case may be, the commission thereof.

# Aiding and abetting in criminal law
[DOMAIN: Tasmanian Criminal Law] [CATEGORY: doctrine] [TYPE: doctrine] [TOPIC: legal distinctions between aiding and abetting] [CONCEPTS: aiding, abetting, mens rea, encouragement, intention] [CITATION: hoc-b018-m003-aid-abet-distinction] [ACT: Criminal Code 1924] [CASE: Giorgianni]

'Aid' is different to 'abet', but they tend to be used interchangeably in many conversations. To 'aid' in the commission of a crime involves a conscious positive decision to act in a way which is designed, and which will in fact enable another to commit the crime or offence. Abet still requires knowledge of what is to transpire, but describes an equally conscious positive decision to be present and supportive in a manner that encourages the other to proceed with that which is proposed.

It is a well-established fact that "merely standing by while a crime is committed will not, of itself, establish guilt" - Gray (1917) 12 Cr App R 244 as cited by Nettlefold J at 4 in *R v Smith* unreported No 4/1979. This case, which dealt with a common law situation of a supervisor directing a driver to drive a defective vehicle leading to a crash, makes clear that parties who are said to aid, abet or instigate an offence must have full "mens rea" (knowledge of what constitutes the offence) and intend by that contribution, support or urging to enable or facilitate the commission of the offence by the principal.

Requirements for liability include:

(i) The commission of the offence by the principal offender;

(ii) That the defendant was present at the time when the offence was committed;

(iii) The defendant knew all the essential facts or circumstances that must be established by the prosecution in order to show the offence was committed by the principal offender;

(iv) That, with that knowledge, he intentionally assisted or encouraged the principal to commit that offence. His intention to assist must be based on that knowledge.

# Criminal liability for common purpose and its implications
[DOMAIN: Tasmanian Criminal Law] [CATEGORY: doctrine] [TYPE: doctrine] [TOPIC: common purpose in prosecution of offences] [CONCEPTS: common purpose, liability, subjective test, offences, mens rea] [CITATION: hoc-b018-m004-common-purpose-liability] [ACT: Criminal Code 1924] [CASE: Shaw v Tasmania]

Where two or more persons form a common intention to prosecute an unlawful purpose in conjunction with one another, and in the prosecution of such purpose a crime is committed of such a nature that its commission was a probable consequence of the prosecution of such purpose, each of them is deemed to have committed the crime. We have examined what occurs when persons are principal offenders, or are deemed to be principal offenders because they have aided, abetted or instigated an offence, but what happens when one offender out of a group commits a crime that wasn't planned? For example, if two people set out to commit an armed robbery, and in the course of that robbery one offender kills someone, is the other offender also guilty of murder? What if they didn't want the victim killed?

Section 4 of the Criminal Code deals with crimes committed in prosecution of a common purpose. This section essentially states that where two people agree to commit a crime, and in the course of committing that crime, another crime is committed by one, then the other is also guilty of that second crime if it was a probable consequence of committing the original crime. Whether the second crime is a probable consequence of the first is assessed objectively, and it is not necessary that the second offender believed that it was probable.

# Criminal Responsibility Under Section 4
[DOMAIN: Tasmanian Criminal Law] [CATEGORY: doctrine] [TYPE: doctrine] [TOPIC: criminal responsibility requires mutual embarkation on a crime] [CONCEPTS: mutual embarkation, criminal responsibility, objective test, common unlawful purpose, probable consequences] [CITATION: hoc-b019-m002-criminal-responsibility] [ACT: Criminal Code Act 1924] [CASE: Shaw v Tasmania]

Not so limited as to apply only to offence b when intent was to commit offence a. "a" can be said to be a probable consequence of intending to commit "a"!

The foundation of criminal responsibility under s 4 is the "mutual embarkation on a crime." The section establishes the ambit of criminal responsibility by participants while carrying out a common unlawful purpose. In contrast to the common law, the Code defines this ambit by requiring a connection of objective probability between the common purpose and the committed crime.

*Interpreted in this way, criminal responsibility will arise when:*

*(a) two or more persons form a common intention to prosecute an unlawful purpose in conjunction with one another, and*

*(b) a crime is committed in the prosecution of such unlawful purpose, and*

*(c) the crime committed is of such a nature that its commission is a probable consequence of the prosecution of the unlawful purpose.*

Provided the requisite connection is established between the unlawful purpose and the crime, there is nothing in the text of the provision which suggests that its operation should be limited by excluding crimes which are included within the intended scope of the unlawful purpose. Obviously, the provision extends the ambit of responsibility beyond this, by applying an objective test of probable consequence. However, this prescribes the outer reach of the provision. It does not expressly or impliedly exclude intended crimes. Self-evidently, a crime which is intended will meet the objective test of "probable consequence."

The criminal responsibility of the person should be determined by application of the test prescribed by s 4 according to its terms. Under that test, a person will be responsible for any crime committed in the prosecution of the common purpose, provided that the crime is of such a nature that its commission was a probable consequence of such prosecution, including those where the test is satisfied because the crime was expressly agreed or contemplated by the common purpose. The intention of the provision is to attribute criminal responsibility to all such crimes, on the basis of their connection to the prosecution of the unlawful purpose, irrespective of the contribution actually made by the person as defined in s 3. The section defines the limits of criminal responsibility arising from the prosecution of common purpose. There is no justification for excluding crimes which fall within that ambit from attribution of criminal responsibility.

As already noted, a consequence of the wider interpretation is the potential for considerable overlap between ss 3 and 4, because in cases in which the crime committed is that which is intended by the participants, criminal responsibility will almost certainly arise under both provisions. We see no difficulty either in principle or in practical application with this outcome. In fact, we would consider it to be the expected and appropriate effect of provisions which attribute criminal responsibility to those complicit in a crime on more than one basis. Certainly, under s 3 it is common for criminal responsibility to arise under more than one provision. We accept that there is a practical danger that relying on both provisions in every case to which both may apply on the facts could lead to unnecessarily complicated directions, and thereby create confusion for juries. Any such problem is capable of amelioration by the exercise of appropriate discretion on the part of the prosecution. As a matter of practice, resort should only be had by prosecutors to s 4 in cases where, on the facts, there may realistically be a need to rely on the extended ambit of the provision in order to attribute criminal responsibility, or where there is clear evidence of a preformed common unlawful purpose, which is additional to inferences drawn from the conduct of the parties in the commission of the crime. In any event, practical issues concerning the potential for overlap of the provisions will not "deny their separate utility."

# Contra Section 12 Surety Arguments
[DOMAIN: Tasmanian Criminal Law] [CATEGORY: doctrine] [TYPE: doctrine] [TOPIC: court considers the capacity of surety and supervision requirements] [CONCEPTS: surety, breach, bail, supervision, safety] [CITATION: hoc-b021-m001-contra-section-12-surety] [ACT: Family Violence Act] [CASE: R v Barrett; Queen v Badger; Tasmania v Wooders]

R v Barrett is a 1985 SA decision referring to the 1843 UK judgment of the Queen v Badger. It is a common law principle applied in the context of a presumption in favour of bail. Neither contemplated or considered the introduction of the Family Violence Act. However violent a departure from common law principles it may be, Section 12 is binding. The court must be satisfied as to the safety and well-being of the complainant. The surety's capacity to pay, alone, should the defendant act contrary to that requirement, is inherently insufficient to discharge that requirement. It is the "supervision" of a defendant by proposed surety that can satisfy the court's, and in that question, the character and antecedents of the proposed surety are not only relevant, but necessary considerations. The court is effectively placing its trust in the surety; it's entitled to consider if that trust is appropriately placed.

At para 22 of JCN, Pearce J said: *"His own home is quite close to the complainant's residence. If he is there, monitoring his movements and conduct would be very difficult. It would achieve little to reduce the risk. His mother's home is in Hobart and would increase the physical separation from the complainant. However, his presence at that home has not, in the past, prevented breach of the order. Nor does his mother's presence seem to prevent breaches. He was at his mother's house with the complainant when he was arrested. The circumstances to which I have referred also reduce my confidence in his mother's capacity as surety to monitor the respondent's behavior and deter any breach."*

At para 14 of *Tasmania v Wooders [2022] TASSC 15*, *"A person who provides a recognizance has an important role, not to be entered into lightly. He or she is, unlike the Court, in a position to supervise obedience to the order and the conditions. A surety undertakes a real and substantial obligation to ensure that the person admitted to bail appears when required and complies with the conditions and must understand the obligations of the accused and the consequences of breach."*

*Secondly, the recognizance also serves to motivate the person released on bail to comply by the assurance that, if they fail to appear or breach a condition, the person who agreed to provide the recognizance will suffer the forfeiture. The accused must know that, if he or she breaks the trust that has been placed in them by a surety, often a family member or friend, the surety will suffer: Mokbel v DPP. The object is to dissuade an accused from breach because of the adverse consequences which might otherwise flow to a surety.*

# Estreatment Applications Against Sureties
[DOMAIN: Tasmanian Criminal Law] [CATEGORY: doctrine] [TYPE: doctrine] [TOPIC: review of section 20 of the Bail Act] [CONCEPTS: surety, doit, application, forfeiture, conditions] [CITATION: hoc-b021-m002-estreatment-applications-against-sureties] [ACT: Bail Act] [CASE: Tasmania v Wooders]

Applications to be made under section 20 of the *Bail Act*. (Section 19 applies to defendants who deposit funds on their own behalf.) The terms of [s 20](http://www.austlii.edu.au/cgi-bin/viewdoc/au/legis/tas/consol_act/ba199441/s20.html) are to be distinguished from [s 19](http://www.austlii.edu.au/cgi-bin/viewdoc/au/legis/tas/consol_act/ba199441/s19.html) which, as I interpret it, applies to a sum deposited by a person admitted to bail with a condition imposed under [s 7(5)(a)](http://www.austlii.edu.au/cgi-bin/viewdoc/au/legis/tas/consol_act/ba199441/s7.html) to secure compliance with the terms of his or her own bail. In such a case, on conviction for breach, a court "must" order forfeiture of the full sum deposited unless the convicted person demonstrates "excessive hardship to that person or the dependants of that person" not relieved by allowing payment by instalments or satisfies the court that it is otherwise just to order forfeiture of part only.

#### Unfairness is a Part-and-parcel of Being a Surety
[Cited in **Tasmania v Wooders [2022] TASSC**]

In R v Crown Court at Maidstone; ex parte Lever [1995] 2 All ER 35, Hoffman LJ said at 41: *"It follows that in one sense the system has unfairness built into it. It may result in persons entirely innocent having to suffer on account of the wrongdoing of another. The courts rely upon the moral pressure which this prospect should apply to the mind of the accused. But the pressure would evaporate if judges were not willing, as a general rule, to harden their hearts against a plea of lack of culpability when it turns out that the surety's trust in the accused was misplaced."*

*As a result, even if a surety does as much as reasonably can be expected, the deterrent effect of a recognizance as it applies to the person released on bail will only work if sureties lose a substantial amount of the sum deposited or promised in the event of breach: R v Agius.*

*In general terms however, a court should take care in attributing significant weight to considerations of financial hardship. To do so would discourage proper consideration of provision of the surety in the first place, would undermine the importance of understanding the genuineness of the surety and would reduce the incentive to discourage breach.*

*The overriding consideration is maintenance of the integrity of the bail system by emphasis on the purposes to be served by provision of surety, the importance of the obligation it imposes and the consequence of breach. For that reason, the greater part of the recognizance should be forfeited.*

***R v AGIUS [2012] SASC 136 (16 July 2012)***

*Held: Alerting authorities is not in itself sufficient to deny forfeiture -- deterrence of person released on bail is an important consideration -- hardship is not ordinarily a sufficient reason to reduce forfeiture but there were special reasons in this case -- application allowed -- monetary sums estreated but amounts of forfeiture reduced.*

[DOMAIN: Tasmanian Criminal Law] [CATEGORY: legislation] [TYPE: legislation] [TOPIC: police power for protective custody] [CONCEPTS: mental illness, protective custody, assessment criteria, reasonable force, entry provisions] [CITATION: hoc-b022-m001-mental-health-protective-custody] [ACT: Mental Health Act 2013] [CASE: None]

Section 17: power for police officer to take a person into protective custody if the police officer reasonably believes that --

(a) the person has a mental illness; and

(b) the person should be examined to see if he or she needs to be assessed against the assessment criteria or the treatment criteria. 

**SCHEDULE 2 - Custody and escort provisions**

**1. Interpretation**

In this Schedule --

**custodian** or **escort**, of a patient, means a police officer, MHO or authorised person who, by or under this Act, is empowered or authorised to take the patient into protective custody or under escort;

**1. General powers, &c., of custodians and escorts**

To take a patient into protective custody or under escort --

(a) the custodian or escort may enlist the assistance of any person, including, if necessary, a police officer; and

(b) the custodian or escort (and any assistant) may use reasonable force against the patient if he or she resists being taken into protective custody or under escort; and

(c) the custodian or escort (and any assistant) may use reasonable force against anyone who may try to prevent the patient from being taken into protective custody or under escort; and

(d) the custodian or escort (if necessary with any assistant) may, without warrant and doing as little damage as possible, enter any premises if the custodian or escort reasonably believes that the patient may be found on those premises; and

(e) the custodian or escort (or any assistant) may take possession of and safeguard any medication, physical aid or other thing that the custodian or escort reasonably believes is or may be necessary to the patient's health, safety or welfare; and

(f) the custodian or escort (or any assistant) may take possession of and safeguard any medication, prescription or other thing that the custodian or escort reasonably believes is or may be relevant to the patient's examination, assessment, treatment or care; and

(g) the custodian or escort may, as circumstances require, transfer physical control of the patient to another custodian or escort or to another police officer, MHO or authorised person (who then has, and may exercise, any powers of the transferring custodian or escort under this clause); and

(h) the custodian or escort is not required to be in close physical proximity to the patient during any examination or assessment.

# Contempt of Court and Case Authority Context
[DOMAIN: Tasmanian Criminal Law] [CATEGORY: case authority] [TYPE: case authority] [TOPIC: contempt in face of the court] [CONCEPTS: contempt of court, judicial authority, evidentiary material, Justices Act, ex parte hearing] [CITATION: hoc-b022-m002-contempt-of-court-authority] [ACT: Justices Act] [CASE: Mansell v Hughes]

In the case of *Mansell v Hughes [2024] TASSC 28 (28 May 2024)*, the court reiterated the principle outlined in Section 25 of the Justices Act, highlighting clear authority for the proposition that something heard but not seen by a judicial officer can constitute contempt in the face of the court. This was supported by precedents such as *Registrar, Court of Appeal v Collins* \[1982\] 1 NSWLR 682 and *Fraser v The Queen* \[1984\] 3 NSWLR 212.

The procedural aspects of hearings further illuminate how courts approach cases involving the defendant's absence. For instance, it is noted that there is no strict requirement for the accused to give evidence before calling other witnesses, although this is a general practice: *RPS v The Queen* (2000) 199 CLR 620 at [8]-[9]. It was highlighted that a trial judge may insist that the defendant must be called first, which raises procedural implications regarding the accused's absence.

If a defendant does not turn up, there are options such as proceeding with an ex parte hearing under rule 31 of the Justices Rules or seeking personal service or a warrant under Rule 32. However, caution is advised when considering ex parte hearings, as the case *Woodward v Moore [2017] TASSC 63* demonstrates that ex parte hearings are not always appropriate.

The Justices Act allows for certain statutory presumptions under Rule 72D, which permits the court to treat matters as prima facie evidence without the accused present. This provides a framework that requires careful scrutiny of the case context and the nature of the complaints being presented. The section presupposes that the complaint will be in proper form, and the legal consequences are based on the content of well-structured complaints rather than unsubstantiated allegations, as discussed in *R v Hush; Ex parte Devanny* (1932) 48 CLR 487.

# Road Safety and Licensing Regulations
[DOMAIN: Tasmanian Criminal Law] [CATEGORY: legislation] [TYPE: legislation] [TOPIC: restricted licences and public interest] [CONCEPTS: restricted licence, public interest, severe hardship, evidentiary provisions, alcohol dependency] [CITATION: hoc-b022-m003-road-safety-licensing-regulations] [ACT: Road Safety (Alcohol and Drugs) Act 1970; Vehicle and Traffic Act 1999] [CASE: R v Stockman; Davies v Deverell; Morton v Scott]

The Road Safety (Alcohol and Drugs) Act 1970 and the Vehicle and Traffic Act 1999 outline the provisions governing restricted licences. Under Section 18(5) of the Vehicle and Traffic Act, punitive measures can accompany the granting of a restricted licence where punishment was imposed. There are statutory bars under Section 19(1A) of the Road Safety Act, which indicate that any offence committed during disqualification periods can disallow applications for returning driving privileges. 

When assessing applications for a restricted licence, the courts consider several factors, including the need for an applicant to establish "severe hardship," which must be convincingly articulated. For example, details about personal circumstances and the unavailability of alternative transport options are vital.

With regard to prior offences, the circumstances surrounding the offending behaviour significantly shape the evaluation of whether granting restricted licences aligns with public safety considerations. There is a public interest in enforcing disqualification laws, which places a significant burden on applicants to demonstrate that their request for driving rights does not undermine the community’s safety or legal integrity.

Cases such as *R v Stockman* underscore that magistrates must carefully weigh the implications of risk to public safety. Similarly, in *Davies v Deverell (1992) 1 Tas R 214*, the court reiterated that evidence presented in support of applications for restricted licences should be properly tested in cross-examination to ensure that the public interest standard governs these legal proceedings.

# Prescribed Belief Standard
[DOMAIN: Tasmanian Criminal Law] [CATEGORY: case authority] [TYPE: case authority] [TOPIC: Standard of Prescribed Belief] [CONCEPTS: prescribed belief, reasonable grounds, objective test, evidence, police database] [CITATION: hoc-b023-m003-prescribed-belief-standard] [ACT: None] [CASE: Tasmania v Hodges [2022] TASSC 45]

\[28\] *"A belief is something more than mere suspicion, but less than actual knowledge based on proof. There must be grounds for the belief, but certainty and proof are not required."*

This point was made by the High Court in *George v Rockett* \[[1990\] HCA 26](http://www6.austlii.edu.au/cgi-bin/viewdoc/au/cases/cth/HCA/1990/26.html); [(1990) 170 CLR 104](http://www6.austlii.edu.au/cgi-bin/LawCite?cit=%281990%29%20170%20CLR%20104) at 116.

> *\"The objective circumstances sufficient to show a reason to believe something need to point more clearly to the subject matter of the belief, but that is not to say that the objective circumstances must establish on the balance of probabilities that the subject matter in fact occurred or exists: the assent of belief is given on more slender evidence than proof. Belief is an inclination of the mind towards assenting to, rather than rejecting, a proposition and the grounds which can reasonably induce that inclination of the mind may, depending on the circumstances, leave something to surmise or conjecture.\"*

\[29\] *Further, the requirement that the belief be reasonable means that it must be based on reasonable grounds. This question is to be judged by the Court by applying an objective test: Samoukovic v Brown \[[1993\] TASSC 78](http://www6.austlii.edu.au/cgi-bin/viewdoc/au/cases/tas/TASSC/1993/78.html), per Zeeman J.*

# Police Belief Basis in Hodges
[DOMAIN: Tasmanian Criminal Law] [CATEGORY: case authority] [TYPE: case authority] [TOPIC: Basis for Belief in Hodges Case] [CONCEPTS: basis of belief, officer's observations, grounds for belief, reasonable grounds, search powers] [CITATION: hoc-b023-m004-belief-basis-hodges] [ACT: None] [CASE: Hodges [2022] TASSC 45]

In ***Hodges***, the basis of the officer's belief was:

- **Intel search showing defendant had drug history.**
- **Demeanour**
- **Physical presentation of the defendant -- fidgety, dilated pupils, dry mouth**

Great para at \[35\]: *"Further, I am satisfied that the basis of the belief as articulated by Constable Whelan, which include her initial suspicions, her observations of the physical indicia displayed by the accused, and the information obtained from the police database during the computer search, provided objectively reasonable grounds for her belief. In applying *[s 29](http://www6.austlii.edu.au/cgi-bin/viewdoc/au/legis/tas/consol_act/moda2001184/s29.html)*, it should be borne in mind that the provision is intended to provide police with powers, including with respect to the search of vehicles, in operational circumstances in which there may be insufficient time or opportunity to obtain a warrant. The constraint on the exercise of those powers is the possession of a prescribed belief. Because it is a power which it is intended to be used in operational circumstances, the formation of the prescribed belief is likely to be in the type of circumstances described by Constable Whelan, that is, a belief which derives from the incremental accumulation of a number of discrete pieces of information, gathered and mentally processed quickly, eventually coalescing in the relevant belief. Provided the belief is actually held, a further constraint on the exercise of the powers is that it must be held on objectively reasonable grounds, but an assessment of objective reasonableness must take account of the circumstances in which the belief is formed. Having regard to all of the circumstances, I am satisfied that Constable Whelan believed that there was a controlled substance in the vehicle, and this belief was based on objectively reasonable grounds."*

# Youth Sentencing Overview Under the Youth Justice Act
[DOMAIN: Tasmanian Criminal Law] [CATEGORY: doctrine] [TYPE: doctrine] [TOPIC: youth sentencing options under the Youth Justice Act] [CONCEPTS: sentencing, rehabilitation, youth offenders, general deterrence, court discretion] [CITATION: hoc-b024-m001-youth-sentencing-options] [ACT: Youth Justice Act 1997 (Tas)] [CASE: DPP v NOP; Director of Public Prosecutions v JSP]

Dismissal is the least severe sentencing option for young offenders sentenced under the Youth Justice Act 1997 (Tas). Dismissal means that a youth is found guilty, but the charge is dismissed and no other sentence is ordered. A conviction cannot be recorded.

A reprimand means that a youth is found guilty and the charge is dismissed and the youth is reprimanded. This is a formal warning by the court. A conviction cannot be recorded.

A dismissal with an undertaking to be of good behaviour is a form of conditional unsupervised release. An undertaking to be of good behaviour is an agreement by a youth to be of good behaviour, which generally means that the youth agrees to adhere to specific conditions set by the court.

Under the sanction of release and adjournment with conditions, the youth is released, and sentencing is postponed for a nominated period (no more than 12 months) on conditions set out by the court. A conviction cannot be recorded. At the conclusion of the adjournment with conditions, if the youth does everything required by the conditions, the youth is discharged from the order. If the youth does not meet the conditions, they may need to return to court, and the court may impose a new order.

For further sentencing measures, probation orders require young people to report to youth justice workers as required. The court can attach special conditions, such as attending school or other educational programs, counselling, or treatment. The youth may also be required to abstain from drinking alcohol or using controlled substances, submit to testing for controlled substances or alcohol, live at a specified address, and/or comply with a curfew.

A community service order can also be an option for young offenders. Detention is described as a last resort; however, it is acknowledged that general deterrence is permissible for serious offending. The Supreme Court has recognized that there are tensions in the aims and purposes of sentencing under the Youth Justice Act 1997 (Tas) that can be difficult to reconcile, particularly regarding serious offences. It has been consistently accepted that while rehabilitation is often a predominant factor, both general and personal deterrence can play a role, especially in serious cases.

For example, in DPP v NOP, a case involving a conviction for rape, Evan J articulated that "whilst rehabilitation is ordinarily the primary consideration for youthful offenders, this does not mean that the Court must disregard the needs of general deterrence and denunciation." Most recently, in Director of Public Prosecutions v JSP, as quoted by Estcourt J in the Court of Criminal Appeal, the established principles concerning juvenile sentencing are reiterated. The principles dictate that the rehabilitation of the offender is always an important, if not the dominant, consideration, and that any sentence should be tailored with greater emphasis on the welfare of the youth; this emphasis on rehabilitation aligns with the broader goal of community protection.

The justification for these sentencing principles is that youthful offenders may not fully appreciate the nature and extent of their criminality and are likely to make ill-considered and immature decisions. Nonetheless, the importance of rehabilitation of a youthful offender is typically far more significant than general deterrence, although instances may arise where just punishment and general deterrence become equally important.

# Definition and Understanding of Harassment under the Family Violence Act
[DOMAIN: Tasmanian Criminal Law] [CATEGORY: doctrine] [TYPE: doctrine] [TOPIC: test is mental element for "harassing" requires intent to act] [CONCEPTS: harassment, Family Violence Act, mental element, conduct, statutory context] [CITATION: hoc-b026-m001-harassment-definition] [ACT: Family Violence Act 2004] [CASE: Cannell v G; G v Cannell [2018] TASSC 55]

Section 4 covers PFVO and FVO definitions of harras-/harassing. It is an exhaustive definition **Cannell v G; G v Cannell [2018] TASSC 55 [para52]** 

The only mental element is that the def intend the ACT that by virtue of the definition in s 4 constitutes harassing, not the act had any particular effect on the person to be protected.

60. "Harass" in its ordinary sense involves conduct committed with the purpose of causing an effect on the person harassed, eg worry, fear or mental anguish: see *Johnson v Collier* [(1997) 142 FLR 409]. However, in my view, the provision of an exhaustive definition of "harassing", by reference to the actions of the alleged harasser, excludes the need to establish that the actions in question have had any particular effect on the person protected by the order, unless expressly so provided in the definition. It must logically follow that there is no requirement that the defendant intend or otherwise foresee the causation of such an effect. These conclusions are confirmed by the inclusion in some of the defined actions of an express requirement that the action occur in circumstances "where it is likely" that there will be some consequence relevant to the protected person. For example, in par (f), it is necessary that the offensive material be left "where it is likely to be found by, given to or brought to the attention of" the protected person. There are similar express requirements contained in pars (g) and (h). There would, of course, be no need to include these specific provisions if an intention to cause or the actual causation of apprehension or fear was already implied in the definition.

61. This construction is consistent with the subject matter and social purpose of the relevant legislation. The provisions in question are concerned with the establishment of firm and viable processes to ensure the protection of persons from persons who probably have committed, and will again commit, family violence [s 16(1)] of the Act. Family violence is recognised as a significant social problem and the Act is directly and expressly directed at dealing with this problem. A family violence order is the primary protective mechanism employed by the Act. [Section 3] provides:

"In the administration of this Act, the safety, psychological wellbeing and interests of people affected by family violence are the paramount considerations."

62. The construction of the Act in respect of the mens rea required for the offence of contravention of an order will be heavily influenced by this statutory context. However, while these considerations weigh against the presumption in favour of proof of intention to commit the offence, they do not completely displace it. ### Heading: Commentary on Family Law Act Amendments
[DOMAIN: Tasmanian Criminal Law] [ACT: Family Law Amendment (Family Violence and Other Measures) Act 2018] [SECTION: 68T; 68R; 68P; 45A] [CITATION: Family Law Amendment Act 2018 - Commentary on Family Law Act Amendments] [CATEGORY: procedure] [TYPE: annotation] [TOPIC: Commentary on Family Law Act Amendments] [CONCEPTS: Family Law Act, family violence orders, judicial discretion, interim orders, child protection]
The *Family Law Amendment (Family Violence and Other Measures) Act 2018* commenced on 31 August 2018.

A copy of the Bill and Explanatory Memorandum can be obtained at https://www.aph.gov.au/Parliamentary_Business/Bills_Legislation/Bills_Search_Results/Result?bId=s1109.

**Amendment to Section 68T -- Special provisions relating to proceedings to make an interim (or interim variation of a) family violence order**

Section 68R of the *Family Law Act 1975* provides that a court of a State or Territory which holds jurisdiction under Part VII of the Family Law Act may, when making a family violence order, revive, vary, discharge, or suspend a range of family law orders to the extent to which they provide for a child to spend time with a person, to ensure they are consistent with the State or Territory family violence order. Subsection 68R(4) restricts the court's power when making an *interim* family violence order to revival, variation or suspension of a family law order.

Section 68T previously provided that where the court, when making an *interim* family violence order or an *interim variation* of a family violence order, revives, varies or suspends an order, injunction or arrangement under section 68R, that revival, variation or suspension ceases to have effect at the earlier of:

(a) the time the interim order stops being in force; and 

(b) the end of the period of 21 days starting when the interim order was made.

The Family Law Amendment (Family Violence and Other Measures) Act has removed the 21-day time limit in paragraph (b) of section 68T and replaced it with:

(b) the time specified in the interim order as the time at which the revival, variation or suspension ceases to have effect; and

(c) the time the order, injunction or arrangement is affected by an order (however described) made by a court, under section 68R or otherwise, after the revival, variation or suspension.

The amendments require judicial officers to consider whether the revival, variation or suspension should expire on a specific date (and if so, when), or whether it should expire when the interim family violence order expires, or when a court makes an alternative order.

These new provisions strengthen the court's ability to protect children and other vulnerable family members by allowing judicial officers to set timeframes according to the particular circumstances of the case. Relevant considerations when making such a decision may include balancing the safety risks to the child arising from having contact with a parent with the maintenance of the child/parent relationship. These changes are intended to better protect children and other vulnerable family members from violence by reducing the risk that can arise from inconsistent orders applying to a child's contact with an allegedly violent parent.

Judicial officers may only change family law orders under section 68R when they have evidence that was not before the court that made the original order (subsection 68R(3)). Evidence must support a finding that changing the family law order is in the best interests of the child concerned including because the family law order is inconsistent with a family violence order in force, or a person, including the child, has been or is likely to be exposed to family violence as a result of the operation of that order (subsection 68R(5)).

### Heading: Commentary on Court Obligations
[DOMAIN: Tasmanian Criminal Law] [ACT: Family Law Amendment (Family Violence and Other Measures) Act 2018] [SECTION: 68P] [CITATION: Family Law Amendment Act 2018 - Commentary on Court Obligations] [CATEGORY: procedure] [TYPE: annotation] [TOPIC: Court Obligations under Family Law Act] [CONCEPTS: court obligations, Family Law Act, family violence order, child protection, legal responsibilities]
**Amendment to Section 68P -- Obligation of court making order or granting an injunction under this Act that is inconsistent with an existing family violence order**

Section 68P of the Family Law Act sets out the obligations of a court when making an order or granting an injunction under the Family Law Act that is *inconsistent with an existing family violence order*. Subparagraph 68P(2)(c)(iii) requires the court, to the extent to which the order or injunction provides for a child to spend time with a person, or expressly or impliedly requires or authorises a person to spend time with a child, to explain the order or injunction to the person protected by the family violence order (if that person is not the applicant or respondent). In some circumstances, the person protected by the family violence order may be a child. Paragraph 68P(2)(d) sets out the matters that the court must include in the explanation, and notes that the explanation must be in language the person is likely to readily understand.

The Family Law Amendment (Family Violence and Other Measures) Act has inserted three new subsections:

- subsection 68P(2A) provides that the court is not required to provide the explanation mandated by subparagraph 68P(2)(c)(iii) to a child if the court is satisfied that it is in the child's best interests not to receive an explanation of the order or injunction;

- subsection 68P(2B) provides that the court is not required to include a particular matter otherwise required to be explained by paragraph 68P(2)(d) if the court is satisfied that it is in the child's best interests for the matter not to be included in the explanation; and 

- subsection 68P(2C) provides that when determining what is in a child's best interests for the purposes of new subsections 68P(2A) and 68P(2B), the court is required to consider the matters in existing subsection 60CC(2), but is not required (but may choose) to consider the matters listed in existing subsection 60CC(3), despite the requirement in subsection 60CC(1).

In most cases, it will be in a child's best interests to understand how court orders apply to them and their family. However, this may not always be the case. In particular, it may not be in the child's best interests to be exposed to the conflict between their parents to the extent necessary for courts to comply with this requirement. In some cases, such as where the child is an infant, it will not be practical to give an explanation.

The provision is designed to ensure that the information children receive from a court is appropriate and does not expose them to further details of family violence which may result in re-traumatisation.

### Heading: Commentary on Summary Decrees
[DOMAIN: Tasmanian Criminal Law] [ACT: Family Law Amendment (Family Violence and Other Measures) Act 2018] [SECTION: 45A] [CITATION: Family Law Amendment Act 2018 - Commentary on Summary Decrees] [CATEGORY: procedure] [TYPE: annotation] [TOPIC: Summary Decrees in Family Law Act] [CONCEPTS: summary decrees, family law, judicial discretion, abuse of process, family violence]
**Insertion of a new Section 45A - Summary decrees**

The Family Law Amendment (Family Violence and Other Measures) Act has inserted a new section 45A into the Family Law Act, which strengthens and codifies family law judges' power to dismiss unmeritorious cases and proceedings that are frivolous, vexatious or an abuse of process. While this does not significantly change the existing powers of the courts exercising family law jurisdiction to dismiss proceedings if they have no reasonable prospect of success, the provision is intended to improve court efficiency by providing greater clarity about when applications can be dismissed by the court.

Subsections 45A(1) and (2) allow the court to make a summary decree in favour of one party, in relation to the whole or part of a proceeding, if satisfied that a party has no reasonable prospect of successfully:

- prosecuting the proceedings or part of the proceedings, or 

- defending the proceedings or part of the proceedings.

Subsection 45A(3) provides that, for the purposes of the section, in determining whether a defence or proceeding has no reasonable prospect of success, proceedings need not be hopeless or bound to fail.

Subsection 45A(4) empowers the court to dismiss all or part of a proceeding if it is frivolous, vexatious or an abuse of process.

Subsection 45A(5) provides that proceedings or a part of proceedings are not to be considered frivolous, vexatious or an abuse of process just because a related application is made and later withdrawn. This is intended to operate as a safeguard against the court misinterpreting the actions of victims of family violence who are litigants in person, who might make an application and then withdraw it for reasons other than the merits of their case, for example, as a result of the power and control dynamics of family violence.

Subsections 45A(6)-(8) provide that:

- the court may make costs orders as it sees fit

- the court may take such action of its own volition, or on the application of a party to the proceedings, and 

- the new section does not limit any powers that the court has apart from this section.

Section 45A has general application. Its use is not limited to matters involving allegations of family violence or other particular categories of disadvantage.

Section 118 of the Family Law Act, which deals with frivolous or vexatious proceedings, has been repealed.

**Other amendments**

The Family Law Amendment (Family Violence and Other Measures) Act has also amended the Family Law Act such that:

- state and territory courts can be prescribed by regulations to have the same jurisdiction in family law parenting matters as that held by state and territory courts of summary jurisdiction under Part VII of the Family Law Act. The new provisions allow for the prescription of classes of proceedings in regulations, in which the prescribed court will have Part VII jurisdiction. It also allows for the prescription of rules of court in regulations, to apply when the prescribed court is exercising family jurisdiction.

It is a requirement that the Attorney-General consult with the relevant state or territory Minister before a court or any other matter is prescribed.

- Courts of summary jurisdiction can hear contested family law property matters without both parties' consent, where the value of the property is up to a new limit to be prescribed in regulations. The new limit will exceed the current value of \$20,000, and different limits can be prescribed for different states and territories (see section 46A).

It is a requirement that the Attorney-General consult with the relevant state or territory Minister before a property value is prescribed.

- Subsection 114(2) of the Family Law Act is repealed. Consequently, it is no longer open to the court to make an order relieving a party to a marriage from an obligation to perform marital services or render conjugal rights. The High Court held in *R v L (1991)* 174 CLR 379 that there is no such obligation under Australian law. Section 114(2) has therefore been effectively redundant since that time.

# Sentencing Considerations in Family Violence Cases
[DOMAIN: Tasmanian Criminal Law] [CATEGORY: doctrine] [TYPE: doctrine] [TOPIC: court considers attitude of complainant] [CONCEPTS: family violence, sentencing, complainant attitude, rehabilitation, statutory provisions] [CITATION: hoc-b028-m001-sentencing-considerations] [ACT: Family Violence Act 2004] [CASE: Brown v Jones [2021] TASSC 58]

As to the attitude of the complainant, to the extent it was suggested it was one of forgiveness, it carries little weight: *Director of Public Prosecutions v Chatters* [2011] TASCCA 8, [21 Tas R 26] at [60], *Menichelli v Tasmania* [2009] TASSC 111, [19 Tas R 299] at [16]. In both cases reference was made to *R v Palu* [2002] NSWCCA 381, [134 A Crim R 174] in which the New South Wales Court of Appeal said the attitude of the victim cannot be allowed to interfere with the proper exercise of the sentencing discretion. The Court went on to say that sentencing proceedings are not a private matter between the victim and the offender, and that a serious crime is a wrong committed against the community at large, and the community itself is entitled to retribution. See also *R v Burton* [2008] NSWCCA 128 at [102]–[106].

That matter of established principle has particular application in cases of family violence. I would respectfully agree with the following comments of Pearce J (with whom Blow CJ and Tennent J agreed) in *Director of Public Prosecutions (Acting) v JCN* [2015] TASFC 13 at [20]:

"The response to family violence is often complex. Family violence offences are not uncommonly accompanied by support of a perpetrator by a victim and reluctance on the part of the victim to assist a criminal prosecution. That is so for a range of possible factors including fear and a wish to preserve relationships, even dysfunctional and violent ones, for the sake of loyalty, affection, companionship, economic and domestic support. Sometimes those motivations are misguided but persist nevertheless. As a result, victims sometimes act in a way that seems to an objective observer to be incongruent and difficult to understand."

# Threshold for Family Violence Orders under Section 29A
[DOMAIN: Tasmanian Criminal Law] [CATEGORY: doctrine] [TYPE: doctrine] [TOPIC: statutory threshold for declarations under s29A] [CONCEPTS: FVO, statutory threshold, risk factors, sentencing, domestic violence] [CITATION: hoc-b028-m002-threshold-s29a] [ACT: Family Violence Act 2004] [CASE: None]

In relation to the declarations under s29A and the associated provisions, Her Honour noted that the statutory threshold is *not a high one*. Both sides should identify when this is triggered and be in a position to address the Court as to whether a declaration is warranted. Section 29A(3) identifies the matters to which the Court is to have regard, but gives a wide scope to that enquiry, noting that s29A(3)(d) allows the Court to look at any other matter which it considers to be relevant.

Her Honour provided a (non-exhaustive) list of matters that she will look at, noting that other Magistrates may consider different factors to be relevant, as follows:
- Gravity and seriousness of the matters on which the defendant is currently being sentenced.
- Risk factors (prior matters, any reports available relating to the defendant).
- Antecedents -- in particular, the gravity of any previous, like offending.
- Whether there is, or has been, more than a single complainant -- a propensity to behave violently in a relationship elevates the risk.
- Escalation in offending.
- Persistence in offending, especially if previous sentences have failed to deter the defendant.
- Personal circumstances.
- Any recognition or insight by the defendant into their behaviour.
- Any attempt to hide the offending conduct.
- A lack of remorse, e.g. if the matter proceeded to hearing.

Her Honour clarified that, from her perspective, she considers the threshold can be met by three (or more) offences being dealt with at court on the same day, even in the absence of prior matters (provided the offences themselves have occurred on different dates). Her Honour also indicated she would be receptive to submissions regarding the length of a declaration, or a further declaration by way of an effective extension (if an existing serial family violence perpetrator is being sentenced for new offending); also to deferring the question of whether to make the declaration or not, if defence are seeking the opportunity to demonstrate rehabilitation.

# Recent Case Law Impacting Sentencing in Family Violence Context
[DOMAIN: Tasmanian Criminal Law] [CATEGORY: case authority] [TYPE: case authority] [TOPIC: Miller v McCullogh factors in family violence sentencing] [CONCEPTS: rehabilitation, sentencing, appeal, family violence, physical violence] [CITATION: hoc-b028-m003-miller-mccullogh] [ACT: Family Violence Act 2004] [CASE: Miller v McCullogh [2024] TASSC 61]

**Miller v McCullogh [2024] TASSC 61 (11 November 2024)** - Held: Def had taken appreciable rehabilitation steps between sentencing and offending, but given severity of physical violence in a FV context, appeal against term of imprisonment was rejected.

Facts: Def charged with multiple assaults -- non-strangulation holding of neck, and emotional abuse. Def had priors for assault against the victim, assault generally and breaching orders. Had pleaded guilty before the pre-set hearing date and had voluntarily engaged in counselling to address issues before sentencing.

As highlighted by counsel for the respondent, the maximum penalty for the Family Violence Act offence is two years' imprisonment. The other three all attract a maximum of 12 months. The applicant had prior convictions for violence, the most recent in April 2020 which led to a suspended prison term. When considering his earlier offending, in particular the family violence offences in 2019, it might be thought that the applicant may have been motivated to seek assistance with his mental health and alcohol abuse issues before being charged in August 2023 with the present matters, and his relationship ending as a result of his conduct.

In any event, the fact remains that he did seek assistance both in relation to his mental health and specifically his alcohol abuse. Those rehabilitation attempts were pursued until he was remanded in custody on 22 April 2024. That fact and the potential continued rehabilitation efforts were not matters that had no significance in the sentencing process. Additionally, the applicant was entitled to some, but in the circumstances no great, credit for his pleas of guilty. The magistrate accepted an expression of remorse but treated it as a "late development". No doubt that was prompted by the initial pleas of not guilty and the very late change. Remorse seems first to have been expressed to Dr Little and given the developing insight in the sessions with him, it is reasonable to accept that it was then genuine.

# Revocation of PFVO vs FVO
[DOMAIN: Tasmanian Criminal Law] [CATEGORY: doctrine] [TYPE: doctrine] [TOPIC: Court considerations for revocation of PFVO compared to FVO] [CONCEPTS: PFVO, FVO, revocation, family violence, court's considerations] [CITATION: hoc-b029-m003-revocation-pfvo-vs-fvo] [ACT: Family Violence Act 2004] [CASE: None]

Not the same as the making of an FVO -- an FVO needs to be satisfied that family violence (not merely an offence) has occurred and that family violence (not merely an offence) will happen again. The revocation of a PFVO has no such indicia. The court may make an FVO at the hearing of revocation of a FVO -- then bound by those considerations -- but if not being asked to make one, or electing its own jurisdiction, the question is only whether to revoke a PFVO.

I accept that this is not necessarily an unreasonable basis for the question to be determined, but that has not been provided for under the ACT. Section 14 should be considered in light of the objects of the Act -- in the administration of the FV Act, the safety, psychological wellbeing, and interests of people affected by family violence are the paramount considerations.

This is consistent with the indicia an Inspector of Tas police must consider, not the convenience of the Respondent. The order was validly made. There can be no further challenge to its validity. Where there is recent physical violence only, in my submission, strong psychological evidence supporting the contention that the protected party's mental wellbeing would be adversely affected by the continuation of the PFVO would justify its revocation -- and none has been submitted.

# Victim Impact Statements: Legislation and Presentation Guidelines
[DOMAIN: Tasmanian Criminal Law] [CATEGORY: doctrine] [TYPE: doctrine] [TOPIC: Victim impact statements under the Sentencing Act 1997] [CONCEPTS: victim impact statement, sentencing, emotional damage, evidentiary limitations, presentation guidelines] [CITATION: hoc-b030-m001-victim-impact-statements] [ACT: Sentencing Act 1997] [CASE: Belbin v Bennett 2011 TASSC]

After a person has been found guilty of, or has pleaded guilty to, an indictable offence the law provides that the victim of the offence can make a victim impact statement (s81A of the Sentencing Act 1997). In respect of a summary offence, a VIS can be provided where death or serious injury has occurred as a result of the offence, or if it is a family violence offence. This is often the only opportunity for the victim to have their say. A VIS is entirely voluntary and if the victim feels it would be more traumatic to go through the process of preparing one then there is no obligation for them to do so. On the other hand, some people find the process of preparing and presenting a VIS therapeutic and want to have a role in the process. The decision whether or not to prepare a VIS can be discussed with a WAS officer who can also assist with the preparation of the document. Victim Support Services can also provide assistance with the preparation of a VIS.

Prosecutors should be aware that a VIS is not evidence to show that a victim is suffering from a psychiatric illness or disorder as a result of the crime. Where necessary, a report should be obtained from a relevant expert.

There are some limitations as to what can be included in a VIS. The statement must be limited to details relating only to the crime of which the offender is convicted. Following are some pointers to assist in the preparation of a VIS. It should include:

• details of any physical injuries caused by the crime and the impact of these injuries

• details of emotional and psychological damage caused by the crime

• details of any changes in behaviour, thoughts, attitudes, coping skills, social life or relationships with others (i.e. how life was before the crime and how it has changed)

• details of the impact of the crime on employment, education or loss of future prospects

• details of the financial impact of the crime

• relevant medical or psychological reports that support the VIS

It should not include:

• any abusive or derogatory comments about the accused

• any impact that is not the result of the crime concerned

• any conduct of which the accused has not been convicted

• any suggestion as to what the sentence should be

Presenting a VIS to the court entails three options. The first option is for the written document to be handed up to the judge for private reading. Some people prefer this because no-one else in the courtroom will hear it. However, it must be borne in mind that a VIS is not an automatically confidential document. A copy must be given to the accused's lawyer so the accused will also see it. Also, judges regularly use extracts from a VIS in their sentencing comments.

The second option is for the victim to read their VIS to the court.

The third option is for the victim to nominate another person to read the VIS on their behalf.

When reading out a VIS, it should be addressed to the judge or magistrate, not directly to the accused. If defence counsel challenge any material in the VIS, it will be necessary to discuss this with the victim. It is possible (though very unusual) that the victim may have to go into the witness box in order to prove the material.

# Interpretation of the Offence of Evading Police
[DOMAIN: Tasmanian Criminal Law] [CATEGORY: doctrine] [TYPE: doctrine] [TOPIC: court considers proper interpretation of evade police provision] [CONCEPTS: evade police, statutory interpretation, apprehension, legislative intention, police powers] [CITATION: hoc-b032-m001-interpretation-evade-police] [ACT: Police Powers (Vehicle Interception) Act 2000] [CASE: A v Roughan [2019] TASSC 17]

The relevant provision of s 11A(1) is as follows:

'(1) The driver of a vehicle must not take action to avoid apprehension or interception by a police officer who is exercising his or her powers or performing his or her functions under any Act.'

The submissions of each counsel were based on what was contended to be the proper interpretation of this provision. The starting point of statutory interpretation must be to give legal effect according to the plain and ordinary meaning of the text, read in the context of the surrounding provisions and the legislative scheme: Project Blue Sky Inc v Australian Broadcasting Authority [1998] HCA 28; (1998) 194 CLR 355. Although the provision is inelegantly drafted, there does not seem to be any great difficulty in attributing meaning to the simple words contained in s 11A(1). Accordingly, in order to establish the offence of evade police, the prosecution must prove that:

(a) the driver of a vehicle;

(b) has taken action to avoid apprehension or interception by a police officer;

(c) who is exercising his or her powers or performing his or her functions under any Act.

The term 'driver' is defined by s 3 of the Act to mean 'a person having control over the steering, movement or propulsion of a vehicle and includes a person riding a vehicle'. This definition suggests a clear nexus between the driving of the vehicle and the taking of action to avoid apprehension or interception by a police officer. This conclusion is consistent with the long title of the Act which is as follows:

'An Act to confer additional powers on police officers to stop and search vehicles and to obtain information concerning the identity of drivers and owners of, and passengers in or on, vehicles used in connection with the commission of certain offences, and to provide for related matters.'

I am not satisfied, however, that the nexus requires the evasion be constituted solely by an act of driving, nor that the evasion and the driving must be contemporaneous and co-extensive. The requirement is that the driver of the vehicle takes action to avoid apprehension or interception. Taking action can involve a single act or a course of conduct. The use of that term without qualification is consistent with a legislative intention to prohibit any form of action which is intended to achieve evasion of a police officer who is acting within the law. The focus is not on the nature or effectiveness of the action, but rather its purpose. It is immaterial that the action is, or is not likely to be successful, in achieving the evasion of the police officer. In the second reading speech which accompanied the amending Act which inserted s 11A into the Act, the then Minister for Police and Emergency Management gave examples of the type of situation in which the section might operate:

'This provision will apply to situations where a driver becomes aware of a police presence and then takes action to avoid apprehension or interception by a police officer. An example of such an offence includes drivers who turn their vehicle around, or pull over and run from their vehicle, when they are approaching a random breath test site, or drives in such a manner as to attempt to evade, elude and or prevent police from speaking to the driver or occupants.'

Accordingly, provided that the Court is satisfied that the action taken by the driver of a vehicle is for the purpose of avoiding apprehension or interception by a police officer, it is not necessary that the evasion occur by the driving of the vehicle, nor that the vehicle is being driven throughout the act of evasion. It is necessary only that the purpose of the action taken be to avoid apprehension or interception by a police officer. The required nexus between the driving of the vehicle and the taking of action to avoid apprehension or interception will be satisfied if the Court can conclude that the action was taken by a person in his capacity as a driver of the vehicle, but this does not necessarily mean that he or she must actually be driving the vehicle at all times, or even at any time, throughout the evasion. Generally, as a practical consequence of this nexus, some contemporaneity can be expected between the actual driving of the vehicle and the action in question. However, because the taking of action can be constituted by a course of conduct, it is not necessary that the entirety of the relevant action be constituted by the driving of the vehicle. Hence, as in the example given by the Minister in the second reading speech, it would be sufficient if the driver has stopped the vehicle and decamped from it in order to avoid apprehension or interception. It is also possible to contemplate circumstances in which the evasive action commences after the driving has come to an end. An example might be a driver who leaves the scene immediately after an accident, for the purpose of evading apprehension by police who are approaching or already present at the scene, but who prior to the driving coming to an end upon the occurrence of the accident was not evading police.

The requirement that the evasion be of a police officer performing his or her functions under any Act, imports a requirement that the taking of action must be for the purpose of avoiding apprehension or interception by an actual police officer. The prosecution must prove that there is a police officer, that he or she is exercising power or performing functions under any Act, and that it is the apprehension or interception by that police officer that the defendant has taken action to evade. It would not be sufficient, for example, that the person believes that police might be in the area when that is not actually the case. On the other hand, it is not necessary for that police officer to have formed an intention to, nor be actually attempting to apprehend or intercept the driver. The power or function does not necessarily need to relate to the apprehension or interception of the driver. The requirement that the driver take action to avoid apprehension or interception by the police officer refers to the subjective purpose of the driver and does not rely upon or circumscribe the intentions of the police officer. It must be independently shown that the police officer is exercising powers or functions under an Act, and provided that is shown, it is irrelevant whether the driver has correctly determined that the police officer intends to apprehend or intercept him or her. The offence is established when the driver of the vehicle takes action for the purpose of avoiding apprehension or interception, whether or not that prospect is, in fact, about to occur.

# Passive Ingestion of Methylamphetamine
[DOMAIN: Tasmanian Criminal Law] [CATEGORY: annotation] [TYPE: annotation] [TOPIC: passive inhalation of methylamphetamine vapour] [CONCEPTS: methylamphetamine, vapour, passive inhalation, scientific evidence, physical characteristics] [CITATION: hoc-b033-m001-passive-inhalation-meth] [ACT: None] [CASE: None]

Methylamphetamine is heated to become a vapour which is then actively breathed into the lungs. It does not exist in the air, like smoke from cigarettes or cannabis -- therefore it cannot be inhaled passively. There is no scientific literature or research into passive methylamphetamine exposure, as it is just not possible to passively inhale methylamphetamine vapour, from our understanding. It will be difficult to write a proof for this, given there is no scientific evidence, but the physical characteristics of methylamphetamine mean that it does not exist in a gas form, it has to be heated to form a vapour.

Further to this, the only other information that I believe could be of help to you is that methylamphetamine hydrochloride (commonly referred to as crystal meth) the form typically "smoked" has a melting point of between 170-175 degrees. Obviously, it will vaporise above this temperature but this gives an indication that methamphetamine is not just floating around in the air of a room at ambient temperature when people are using it. It will, in fact, easily condense on the cooler parts of the glass smoking devices used before it has any chance of "escaping" into a room -- at any significant/detectable level. Meth and THC differ considerably in how they are "smoked". Cannabis is typically literally burnt as per a cigarette so that the smoke is inhaled/exhaled. Meth is heated until it changes from a solid to a liquid to a gas and this is what is inhaled. Smoke from a cannabis joint can be long lasting (given the right conditions e.g. lack of ventilation), meth vapour is not the same.

# Lawful Excuse in Relation to Dangerous Articles
[DOMAIN: Tasmanian Criminal Law] [CATEGORY: doctrine] [TYPE: doctrine] [TOPIC: lawful excuse for possession of dangerous articles] [CONCEPTS: lawful excuse, dangerous article, recreation, intent, High Court] [CITATION: hoc-b036-m002-lawful-excuse-dangerous-articles] [ACT: Police Offences Act 1935] [CASE: Craig v Lowrey; Taikato v R; Holmes v Hatton]

#### Dangerous Article (Lawful Excuse)

***Craig v Lowrey* \[2012\] TASMC 41**

The overall effect of the defendant's explanations was that he kept the machete for recreational or farming purposes unrelated to attack or self-defence.

In *Taikato v R* \[1996\] HCA 28, the majority of the High Court in relation to a similar charge discussed the meaning of "lawful purpose". They held that as a general rule interpreting "lawful purpose" in a legislative provision to mean a purpose that is not forbidden, rather than positively authorised by law, is usually preferred. This is because statutes are interpreted in accordance with the presumption that parliament does not take away existing rights unless it does so expressly or by necessary implication. In this matter it was not argued by prosecution that using the machete for the purposes of a tool, as the defendant describes, was in itself unlawful. It is not prohibited by law, and I find that such use would be a lawful activity.

Two decisions of the South Australian Supreme Court assist with the principles to be applied in proving "lawful excuse" in the context of this type of charge. I will set out relevant passages from those decisions.

In *Holmes v Hatton* \[18 SASR 412\], Wells J stated at p418:

It seems to me that the expression "without lawful excuse" obliges a Court to examine all the circumstances that characterize the purpose and reasons for which the weapon is carried, and to determine, as a matter of fact and degree whether, reasonably appraised, those purposes and reasons constitute lawful excuse or not.

His Honour further stated as p419:

> *Where an offensive weapon is carried knowingly, lawful excuse or its absence is ordinarily to be judged by a consideration of the purpose or purposes, or the reason or reasons, for which the weapon is being carried. (If it should be -- and I leave this point for consideration when it directly arises -- that a man may "carry" a weapon within the meaning of the section even where he is unaware that he is carrying it -- for example, where he is helping to carry another's luggage which, unknown to him contains a gun -- absence of purpose or reason and an inadvertent carrying may amount to lawful excuse).*
>
> *The purpose or reasons for which an alleged offender was carrying a weapon are to be examined and assessed having regard to the united force of all the circumstances put together. If the sole purpose or reason for which the weapon is carried is plainly unlawful or is otherwise contrary to the section, the carrier cannot claim lawful excuse.*
>
> *Where the sole purpose or reason is lawful, there will be lawful excuse. Where there is a duality of purpose or reason, no hard and fast rule can provide a solution. The court, in determining the issue, must, in my opinion, decide a question of fact and degree, and must, in particular, ask whether that which, ex facie and taken in isolation, may be regarded as an unlawful purpose or reason, was, in the circumstances, so transient, so remotely related to the defendant's conduct, so speculative, so unlikely to become translated into action, so subsidiary -- in short, so unimportant or insignificant, as to leave the alleged offender with lawful excuse.*
>
> *The foregoing discussion, it will be observed, has omitted any reference to onus of proof. The application of the provision as to onus of proof should cause little difficulty in practice, but occasional cases, in which dual purpose is under examination, may arise where it will be necessary to keep steadily in mind what the defendant is obliged by [s. 15](http://www.austlii.edu.au/au/legis/tas/consol_act/ea200180/s15.html) to prove.*
>
> *Plainly, given that all other elements are proved, if only a single purpose or reason is in issue, and if the defendant proves on the balance of probabilities primary facts that, in law, amount to lawful excuse, he will be acquitted. If proof falls short of that, he must be convicted. If the evidence as a whole raises a question of dual purpose or reason, the matter will, to my mind, stand thus. Where the evidence as a whole includes evidence suggesting an unlawful purpose or reason for carrying as well as of a lawful purpose or reason, in order to acquit the defendant the court must find itself persuaded that, having regard to the cogency with which the relevant facts and circumstance are proved by the evidence as a whole, the unlawful purpose or reason was so unimportant and insignificant that there probably was lawful excuse.*

# Abandonment and Reasonable Steps to Find Owner
[DOMAIN: Tasmanian Criminal Law] [CATEGORY: doctrine] [TYPE: doctrine] [TOPIC: court considers requirement for reasonable belief about ownership] [CONCEPTS: abandonment, theft, reasonable steps, dishonest belief, possession] [CITATION: hoc-b037-m001-abandonment-reasonable-steps] [ACT: Criminal Code Act 1924] [CASE: *Maingay v Charles [2009] TASSC 98; R v White (1912) 7 Cr App R 266; Ellerman's Wilson Line Ltd. v Webster [1952] 1 Ll.L.R. 179; Alexander Gordon MacDonald [1983] 8 A Crim R 248]

Property that has been abandoned cannot be stolen (see *post ss* 18 -- 24). If property has not been abandoned, but the defendant believes that it has, under the old law he could not be convicted of theft, whether his belief was reasonable or unreasonable. This principle is supported by cases such as *R v White* (1912) 7 Cr App R 266 and *Ellerman's Wilson Line Ltd. v Webster* [1952] 1 Ll.L.R. 179, D.C. It is submitted that this is still the law. Whether or not a mistake of fact has to be reasonable (see ss 17-4 *et seq.*), there would be no evidence that the defendant was acting dishonestly.

In the case of ***Maingay v Charles [2009] TASSC 98***, the respondent's stated belief was in effect that he did not believe he could discover the owner by any reasonable means. While there is no stated requirement in s226 that the belief itself be a reasonable one, it would make a nonsense of the legislation were an alleged offender to simply be able to state that he did not hold the requisite belief, notwithstanding that that stated belief might be entirely inconsistent with the surrounding circumstances, and for that statement to be determinative. Of course, the belief referred to in s226 is one that an owner can be discovered by "reasonable means."

Maingay also cites *Alexander Gordon MacDonald [1983] 8 A Crim R 248*, where the Court of Criminal Appeal of New South Wales considered how the state of mind of a finder of property is to be established when the finder has volunteered no information pointing to their state of mind at the relevant time. The Court (Lee, Maxwell, and Yeldham JJ) observed (at p251):

"The guilty mind or animus furandi requisite in larceny by finding is thus the belief of the finder that the owner can be found, and unless that belief is established beyond reasonable doubt, a conviction for larceny is not open. The Crown of course carries the onus of proving that belief beyond reasonable doubt, and there is no onus on the accused in any circumstances to prove that he did not have such a belief or that he believed the goods had been abandoned, or that the owner could not be found (May v O'Sullivan [1955] HCA 38; (1955) 92 C.L.R. 654). How then is the requisite belief in the finder to be established when the finder has volunteered no information pointing to the state of his mind at the relevant time, as is the case here? In Russell on Crime (12th edition) the learned author, at p1014, states: 

"In cases of taking on finding some of the strongest circumstances to rebut the implication that such taking was felonious are those which show that [the taker] endeavoured to discover the true owner and kept the goods till it might reasonably be supposed that the true owner could not be found. The finder's belief, in our view, is to be inferred from the facts and circumstances surrounding the finding and the taking of the goods, and in this respect regard may be had not only to what the finder does in relation to the goods but also what he does not do that might reasonably be regarded as consistent with the actions of an honest man finding goods. Did the finder examine it closely to see if it gave any clue to its owner by name tag or otherwise? What avenues were reasonably open to the finder to locate the owner? A person finding goods may be taken to know that the person who has lost those goods may well retrace his steps with a view to recovering them, and so the leaving of the finder's name and address with someone at the place of discovery may be one means of locating the owner. It is common knowledge in our community, and the finder would know, that the police will receive lost property handed in and take care of it and accordingly the finder would think that the loser might make an enquiry of the police in the area where he believes he may have lost his property. It is common knowledge that newspapers publish lost and found columns. These, and perhaps other considerations would ordinarily be present to the mind of an honest person finding property in a suburb of Sydney. Each case must be looked at according to its own facts, and the place where the property is found and the nature of the property will, in most instances, readily indicate the avenues that are reasonably open to find the owner."

# Mental Elements in Theft: Dishonesty and Intent
[DOMAIN: Tasmanian Criminal Law] [CATEGORY: doctrine] [TYPE: doctrine] [TOPIC: test for dishonesty involves objective and subjective elements] [CONCEPTS: dishonesty, intent, theft, belief, criminal code] [CITATION: hoc-b037-m002-mental-elements-theft] [ACT: Criminal Code Act 1924] [CASE: *Peters (1998) 192 CLR 493; Macleod (2003) ALJR 1047; Jovanovic; Lowe v Hooke; Holloway; Moore v Howe [2017] TASMC 10*]

Two mental elements are important in the context of theft – dishonesty and intent to permanently deprive.

Dishonestly under s226(1) is evaluated by a test that is both objective and subjective, and it is now clear that courts will apply the *Feely* test without the *Ghosh* gloss. This was accepted by the High Court in *Peters* (1998) 192 CLR 493 and *Macleod* (2003) ALJR 1047. In determining dishonesty, it is necessary to:

- Identify the knowledge, belief, or intent which is said to render the relevant act dishonest; 
- Instruct the jury to decide whether the accused had that knowledge, belief or intent and, if so, to determine whether, on that account, the act was dishonest; and 
- Direct the jury that, in determining whether the conduct of D was dishonest, the standard is that of ordinary and decent people. 

There is no additional requirement that D realized he or she was dishonest (*Jovanovic*).

The intent to permanently deprive requires that the accused has an intent to assume dominion over the goods in such a manner as to deny the owner access to their property, as clarified in *Lowe v Hooke*. It is not sufficient for the intent to merely encroach upon the rights of the owner (as established in *Holloway*, using but not permanently depriving).

Notably, there is no limitation period on charges for stealing, as highlighted in *Moore v Howe [2017] TASMC 10* where it was noted that "Stealing is an indictable offence under s 234 of the Code and is not governed by that provision, as it deals with 'a simple offence that is not an indictable offence'. There is no limit for the bringing of this complaint."

# Reasonably Believed - Analysis of Officer's Belief
[DOMAIN: Tasmanian Criminal Law] [CATEGORY: doctrine] [TYPE: doctrine] [TOPIC: belief must amount to acceptance to exclusion of other propositions] [CONCEPTS: belief, suspicion, police powers, reasonable grounds, subjective assessment] [CITATION: hoc-b038-m001-reasonably-believed] [ACT: Police Offences Act 1935] [CASE: Lane v Jones [2012] TASMC 11, Parker v Wood [1993] TASSC 26, Winwood v Burk [2002] TASSC 54, Burnett v Brown (1929) 24 Tas LR 23, Rolls v Richardson [2014] TASMC 12, Cotton v Ramm [1976] 16 SASR 107, Hibble v Phegan]

Evidence needs to amount to acceptance by the officer - to the exclusion of other propositions - that the property was stolen or unlawfully obtained at para [23] **Lane v Jones [2012]**. This question is to be assessed subjectively. I note that the requisite state of mind is something more than mere suspicion.

*Lane v Jones [2012] TASMC 11*, Deputy Chief Magistrate Daly analysed the meaning of this term as it appears in s 39, Police Offences Act. He concluded that a belief must amount "to acceptance -- to the exclusion of other propositions --" that the relevant state of affairs exists. With respect, I agree with His Honour's analysis and adopt that test in assessing whether Sergeant Orr held the requisite belief.

The Act, s.39(1) provides that a person must not have possession or control of any property that is reasonably believed by a police officer to have been stolen or unlawfully obtained. The Supreme Court has not dealt with any case charged under the amended provision, which came into force on 17 December 2001.

In Parker v Wood [1993] TASSC 26, Slicer J dealt with the test applicable to the words "reasonably believes" in the Road Safety Alcohol & Drugs Act 1970, s.8. Slicer J stated in relation to that particular requirement, that the reason a constable must hold a reasonable belief is to prevent random and capricious exercises of police powers. The Second Reading Speech is of no assistance whatsoever. However, Slicer J's comments are apposite here in relation to police capriciously or unreasonably charging persons under this provision. Slicer J held that the test in relation to the phrase "reasonably supposes" is that in George v Rockett [1990] HCA 26; (1990) 170 CLR 104 at 112:

*"When a statute prescribes that there must be 'reasonable grounds' for a state of mind - including suspicion and belief - it requires the existence of facts which are sufficient to induce that state of mind in a reasonable person."*

And at p116:

*"The objective circumstances sufficient to show a reason to believe something need to point more clearly to the subject matter of the belief, but that is not to say that the objective circumstances must establish on the balance of probabilities that the subject matter in fact occurred or exists: the assent of belief is given on more slender evidence than proof. Belief is an inclination of the mind towards assenting to, rather than rejecting, a proposition and the grounds which can reasonably induce that inclination of the mind may, depending on the circumstances, leave something to surmise or conjecture."*

In relation to the degree of suspicion that might be required, in Winwood v Burk [2002] TASSC 54, Underwood J, as he then was, said:

In O'Sullivan v Tregaskis [1948] SASR 12, Napier CJ said at 16:

*"It is manifestly impossible to enumerate or define all the circumstances that may reasonably attract suspicion to the property, but, for this purpose, regard may be had to the nature of the property, to the circumstances in which it has been found, and to the behaviour of the defendant, with respect to it. (Ex parte Patmoy (1944) 44 SR (NSW) 351 at p 357; LePoidevin v Hudson [1935] SASR 223, at p 230)."*

*"Speaking generally, it seems to me that suspicion is commonly, and not unreasonably, incited by the impression of abnormality or incongruity in the thing being in the possession of the person who has it, or in his dealings with it."*

In Burnett v Brown [(1929) 24 Tas LR 23], Clark J, at 34, said with respect to the Police Act 1905, s16, an earlier Tasmanian enactment similar to s.39:

*"The words 'suspicion' and 'suspect' convey the idea of doubt and mistrust as much as belief, a doubting and mistrusting inclination to believe. The inclination may exist in various degrees according to the cogency of the evidence, but the idea of doubt and mistrust is always present."*
