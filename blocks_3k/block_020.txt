## Seeking Bail conditions/ restrictions:

Courts can also grant conditional bail (*Justices Act*). The o**nus is** on the Crown to affirmatively persuade the court to exercise its discretion. The judge must be satisfied of the necessity to impose conditions and cannot impose general conditions; regard must be had to the circumstances of the case, and the imposition of any conditions must be linked to one of the primary considerations in bail applications (*Levy v Strickland*). A court cannot simply impose **standard conditions** by virtue of the offence (as in Brown -- the judge initially imposed "the usual condition" in relation to not driving with alcohol in his body, for defendant charged with drink driving). Rather, the court must consider the individual circumstances of the case.

*However, an order for bail may be made subject to such other conditions as the judicial officer thinks desirable in the interests of justice: [s 7(4).](http://www.austlii.edu.au/cgi-bin/viewdoc/au/legis/tas/consol_act/ba199441/s7.html) Conditions may serve a number of purposes. They may be directed not only to reducing the risk that the person admitted to bail will abscond. They may also be directed to other matters relevant to the decision to grant or refuse bail, including the risk that a person will use the opportunity of bail to continue to offend, may present a risk to a person or persons, or influence potential witnesses.* ***Tasmania v Wooders \[2022\] TASSC 15 (28 February 2022)***

The case concerned the determination of bail by reference to section 12 of the  Family Violence Act, in the context of the defendant having failed to appear in court on FV matters and having then been arrested on a warrant. Usefully for our purposes, the Court (Pearce J and Porter AJ) noted the following, at \[7\]:

\"We would accept the submission of counsel for the respondent that it was not to the point that the appellant had previously been granted bail. It was for the appellant to satisfy this Court, on the material presented on the hearing of the appeal, that the grant of bail should be made.\"

This statement would appear to be authority for the principle that, in any case where bail is called into question again subsequent to having been granted (by police or by a court, and whether by reason of the defendant having failed to appear or by breaching protective orders, or both), the Court determining the section 12 question is entitled - and indeed required - to consider afresh all of the pending allegations of family violence. I have made submissions previously when in defence that the question is largely confined to fresh allegations, or at least that those allegations should be the focus of the enquiry - I have had some degree of success with this argument, but it would seem there is now clear authority to the contrary.

For completeness, I note that the defendant in the case before the Full Court was granted bail. The judgment makes clear however that this decision was heavily influenced by the absence of any allegations of further FV offending or breaches of protective orders.

## Surety

**There is a positive duty on a surety:**

**\[14\]...** *The surety is required to take positive steps.
