### Express and implied threats

Recent HCA authority **The King v Anna Rowan - A Pseudonym \[2024\] HCA 9 (13 March 2024)**

37. The necessity for the accused to be subject to a threat of infliction of the relevant form of harm if the accused fails to commit the acts that constitute the offence charged has been a consistent feature of the consideration of the common law of duress by intermediate courts of appeal in the common law jurisdictions in this country.[**^\[31\]^**](https://www.austlii.edu.au/cgi-bin/viewdoc/au/cases/cth/HCA/2024/9.html#fn31)

<!-- -->

38. The nature of the requisite threat is illustrated by the facts of *R v Dawson*[**^\[32\]^**](https://www.austlii.edu.au/cgi-bin/viewdoc/au/cases/cth/HCA/2024/9.html#fn32) and *R v Lorenz*.[**^\[33\]^**](https://www.austlii.edu.au/cgi-bin/viewdoc/au/cases/cth/HCA/2024/9.html#fn33) In *Dawson*, the accused escaped from prison but led evidence that he did so because he feared for his life after receiving threats from other prisoners. The Full Court held that duress was not open because the threats were not made on the basis that, if he did not escape, he would be subject to violence.[**^\[34\]^**](https://www.austlii.edu.au/cgi-bin/viewdoc/au/cases/cth/HCA/2024/9.html#fn34) In *Lorenz*, the accused robbed a supermarket employee with a knife after her de facto partner threatened to kill her if she did not obtain enough money to re-register his car. The accused was pregnant at the time.[**^\[35\]^**](https://www.austlii.edu.au/cgi-bin/viewdoc/au/cases/cth/HCA/2024/9.html#fn35) After noting *Hurley* and *Dawson*, Crispin J held that the defence of duress was not available as the accused\'s de facto partner did not direct her to commit the robbery.[**^\[36\]^**](https://www.austlii.edu.au/cgi-bin/viewdoc/au/cases/cth/HCA/2024/9.html#fn36)

<!-- -->

39. However, neither *Dawson* nor *Lorenz *suggests that a threat to inflict the relevant form of harm if the accused fails to commit the acts constituting the charge cannot be implied. In *Hurley*, Smith J postulated the scenario of armed men taking possession of a house at gunpoint and sending the accused to commit a crime on their behalf while retaining a hostage such that \"the threat to the hostage\'s life, *whether it is formulated in words or left unsaid*, may be held to be, during the execution of the commission, sufficiently present and continuing, imminent and impending to found the defence of duress\" (emphasis added).[**^\[37\]^**](https://www.austlii.edu.au/cgi-bin/viewdoc/au/cases/cth/HCA/2024/9.html#fn37)

<!-- -->

40. The references in *Hurley* to the threat being \"present and continuing, imminent and impending\" do not require that all the conduct relied upon as giving rise to the threat be engaged in immediately prior to the crime being committed. Instead, those statements should be taken as conveying a requirement that the threat conform with the description of the equivalent aspect of duress noted in other cases, namely that the threat be \"operative\"[**^\[38\]^**](https://www.austlii.edu.au/cgi-bin/viewdoc/au/cases/cth/HCA/2024/9.html#fn38) or \"effective\"[**^\[39\]^**](https://www.austlii.edu.au/cgi-bin/viewdoc/au/cases/cth/HCA/2024/9.html#fn39) at the time the accused committed the acts that constitute the offence charged. The relevant threat does not have to immediately precede the accused\'s criminal act if there was no reasonable opportunity to avoid committing the offence in the meantime (that being the issue raised by element (viii) in *Hurley*).[**^\[40\]^**](https://www.austlii.edu.au/cgi-bin/viewdoc/au/cases/cth/HCA/2024/9.html#fn40)

### AID OR ABET

'Aid' is different to 'abet', but they tend to be used interchangeably in many conversations.

To 'aid' in the commission of a crime involves a conscious positive decision to act in a way which is designed, and which will in fact enable another to commit the crime or offence. Abet still requires knowledge of what is to transpire, but describes an equally conscious positive decision to be present and supportive in a manner that encourages the other to proceed with that which is proposed.

It is a well established fact that "...*merely standing by while a crime is committed will not, of itself, establish guilt*" - Gray (1917) 12 Cr App R 244 as cited by Nettlefold J at 4 in *R v Smith* unreported No 4/1979.

***Giorgianni* (1985) 156 CLR 47**

This case, which dealt with a common law situation of a supervisor directing a driver to drive a defective vehicle leading to a crash, makes clear that parties who are said to aid, abet or instigate an offence, must have full "mens rea" (knowledge of what constitutes the offence) and intend by that contribution, support or urging to enable or facilitate the commission of the offence by the principal.

Requirements:

(i) The commission of the offence by the principal offender;

(ii) That the defendant was present at the time when the offence was committed;

(iii) The defendant knew all the essential facts or circumstances that must be established by the prosecution in order to show the offence was committed by the principal offender;

(iv) That, with that knowledge, he intentionally assisted or encouraged the principle to commit that offence. His intention to assist must be based on that knowledge.

**Aid or Abet**

The defendant was convicted of stealing as an aider by being in company with some males who attended two bottleshops whilst they stole some alcohol. The conviction was appealed by defence counsel.

At 12 Tennet J stated;

In *Randall v R; Farmer v R* \[2004\] TASSC 42 at par40, Cox CJ dealt with the concepts of aiding and abetting. He said:

\"40 The concepts of aiding and of abetting, as the learned trial judge acknowledged, can overlap. In respect of aiding, the *Criminal Code, s3,* requires that there be an act done or an omission made for the purpose of enabling or aiding the principal offender to commit the crime. Abetting requires not merely wilful encouragement by the abettor, but the communication of that encouragement to the offender with the consequence that the offender is in fact encouraged. In either case, some active step must be taken with intent to bring about the commission of the crime by the principal offender. In most cases of aiding, some physical act or deliberate omission can be readily identified and in many cases of abetting, the communication of encouragement may take an identifiable physical form such as gestures or spoken words.

In *R v Coney & Ors* (1882) 8 QBD 534, Hawkins J said at 557 - 558:

\'In my opinion, to constitute an aider and abettor some active steps must be taken by word, or action, with the intent to instigate the principal, or principals. Encouragement does not of necessity amount to aiding and abetting, it may be intentional or unintentional, a man may unwittingly encourage another in fact by his presence, by misinterpreted words, or gestures, or by his silence, on non-interference, or he may encourage intentionally by expressions, gestures, or actions intended to signify approval. In the latter case he aids and abets, in the former he does not. It is no criminal offence to stand by, a mere passive spectator of a crime, even of a murder. Non-interference to prevent a crime is not itself a crime. But the fact that a person was voluntarily and purposely present witnessing the commission of a crime, and offered no opposition to it, though he might reasonably be expected to prevent and had the power so to do, or at least to express his dissent, might under some circumstances, afford cogent evidence upon which a jury would be justified in finding that he wilfully encouraged and so aided and abetted.\'

Mere presence, even if it has the effect of encouraging the principal offender to commit the crime and is accompanied on the part of the accomplice by an intention to render assistance, if required, is not sufficient (*R v Jones & Mirrless* (1977) 65 Cr App R 250 at 252; *Roughley v R* (supra) at 12).\"

At 18 Tennet J stated;

This was clearly, on the material before the learned magistrate, not a case of just mere presence of the applicant. It involved his travelling in a car for some distance with the other three males, and knowing they were going to go into bottle shops and steal alcohol. It involved the applicant actually going into the two bottle shops with the other three males, standing nearby in one case in full view while the youth or youths physically took the alcohol and concealed it, remaining with them at Burnie when one of the youths activated an alarm, and then, in each case, leaving the bottle shops with the males and getting into a car with them and the stolen alcohol. The finding by the learned magistrate that the applicant actively participated in these thefts, as opposed to his simply being incidentally present, was, in my view, open to him on the evidence.

Best case to look at is Clarke v Tas.

*In the judgment of Clarke v Tasmania \[2013\] TASCCA 11 Porter J states "It seems to me that the accepted approach in this State is that if two or more persons form a common purpose to commit crime X, and crime Y is committed, the liability of each is to be first assessed under s3, with resort had to s4 if necessary". The essential difference between the two sections is that s3 the test is subjective in that the Defendant must have the full mens rea of the latter crime, while s4 has an objective test provided the latter crime was 'a probable consequence' of carrying out their original unlawful purpose.*

**4. Crimes committed in prosecution of common purpose**

Where 2 or more persons form a common intention to prosecute an unlawful purpose in conjunction with one another, and in the prosecution of such purpose a crime is committed of such a nature that its commission was a probable consequence of the prosecution of such purpose, each of them is deemed to have committed the crime.

We have examined what occurs when persons are principal offenders, or are deemed to be principal offenders because they have aided, abetted or instigated an offence, but what happens when one offender out of a group commits a crime that wasn't planned? For example, if two people set out to commit an armed robbery, and in the course of that robbery one offender kills someone, is the other offender also guilty of murder? What if they didn't want the victim killed?

Section 4 of the *Criminal Code* deals with crimes committed in prosecution of a common purpose. This section essentially states that where two people agree to commit a crime, and in the course of committing that crime another crime is committed by one, then the other is also guilty of that second crime if it was a probable consequence of committing the original crime.
