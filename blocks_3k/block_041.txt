# Evidence Types

## Circumstantial evidence 

\[8\] Circumstantial evidence is evidence which proves or tends to prove a fact or set of facts from which the fact to be proved may be inferred. Circumstantial evidence can prove a fact beyond reasonable doubt only if all other reasonable hypotheses are excluded. **Doney v R \[1990\] HCA 51; (1990) 171 CLR 207**

Circumstantial evidence pointing towards the guilt of the defendant. In order for such evidence to prove a fact beyond reasonable doubt, that conclusion is only open if all other reasonable hypotheses consistent with innocence are excluded: see *Doney v The Queen* [\[1990\] HCA 51](http://www.austlii.edu.au/cgi-bin/viewdoc/au/cases/cth/HCA/1990/51.html); [\[1990\] 171 CLR 207](http://www.austlii.edu.au/cgi-bin/LawCite?cit=%5b1990%5d%20171%20CLR%20207) at 211. Guilt must be the only hypothesis reasonably open.

Any such explanation or hypothesis must be more than fanciful or far-fetched. The hypothesis, to be reasonable, must possess some degree of acceptability or credibility on the evidence before me. (See *Howlett v Budd* [\[2003\] TASSC 49)](http://www.austlii.edu.au/cgi-bin/viewdoc/au/cases/tas/TASSC/2003/49.html)

Even if the court is unconvinced by the evidence of the defendant - being his broad, simple, blanket denial of any knowledge of involvement -- the court must still be satisfied beyond reasonable doubt of the prosecution case before It can convict. As I have said, the defendant bears no onus to advance a reasonable hypothesis consistent with innocence. (See *R v Betancur-Galvis *[\[2003\] NSWCCA 333](http://www.austlii.edu.au/cgi-bin/viewdoc/au/cases/nsw/NSWCCA/2003/333.html); [\[2003\] 142 A Crim R 527).](http://www.austlii.edu.au/cgi-bin/LawCite?cit=%5b2003%5d%20142%20A%20Crim%20R%20527)

Circimstatial DNA Evidence:

**Roughan v Haines \[2019\] TASMC 7 (25 November 2019)**

\[15\] In that regard, even if the defendant's evidence itself does not itself create a reasonable doubt, I must necessarily assess the DNA evidence with care. Where DNA material is deposited, and how it is deposited must be considered: see *Fitzgerald v The Queen* [\[2014\] HCA 23](http://www.austlii.edu.au/cgi-bin/viewdoc/au/cases/cth/HCA/2014/23.html) as an example, albeit one with a very different factual framework. Here, however, the nature of the sample and its position, and when it was found is such that, absent some other evidence, in my view no reasonable hypothesis consistent with innocence is apparent to me that would cause me to have any reasonable doubt as to the defendant's guilt.

In this it must be steadfastly kept in mind that the Def Haines bears no onus.

### **Circimstantial evidence "long thread"**

**High Court in *R v Edwards *[\[1993\] HCA 63](http://www.austlii.edu.au/cgi-bin/viewdoc/au/cases/cth/HCA/1993/63.html); [(1993) 178 CLR 193](http://www.austlii.edu.au/cgi-bin/LawCite?cit=%281993%29%20178%20CLR%20193), [68 A Crim R 349](http://www.austlii.edu.au/cgi-bin/LawCite?cit=68%20A%20Crim%20R%20349), a case which deals with post offence conduct in the form of lies:**

The jury do not have to conclude that the accused is guilty beyond reasonable doubt in order to accept that a lie told by him exhibits a consciousness of guilt. They may accept that evidence without applying any particular standard of proof and conclude that, when they consider it together with the other evidence, the accused is or is not guilty beyond reasonable doubt.
