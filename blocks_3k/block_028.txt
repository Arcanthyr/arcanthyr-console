### Court Considerations (as discussed by Magi Edwards). 

In relation to the declarations under s29A and the associated provisions, Her Honour noted that the statutory threshold is *not a high one*. Both sides should identify when this is triggered and be in a position to address the Court as to whether a declaration is warranted. Section 29A(3) identifies the matters to which the Court is to have regard, but gives a wide scope to that enquiry, noting that s29A(3)(d) allows the Court to look at any other matter which it considers to be relevant.

**Her Honour provided a (non-exhaustive) list of matters that she will look at, noting that other Magistrates may consider different factors to be relevant, as follows:**

- Gravity and seriousness of the matters on which the defendant is currently being sentenced.

- Risk factors (prior matters, any reports available relating to the defendant).

- Antecedents -- in particular, the gravity of any previous, like offending.

- Whether there is, or has been, more than a single complainant -- a propensity to behave violently in a relationship elevates the risk.

- Escalation in offending.

- Persistence in offending, especially if previous sentences have failed to deter the defendant.

- Personal circumstances.

- Any recognition or insight by the defendant into their behaviour.

- Any attempt to hide the offending conduct.

- A lack of remorse, e.g. if the matter proceeded to hearing.

Her Honour clarified that, from her perspective, she considers the threshold can be met by three (or more) offences being dealt with at court on the same day, even in the absence of prior matters (provided the offences themselves have occurred on different dates). Her Honour also indicated she would be receptive to submissions regarding the length of a declaration, or a further declaration by way of an effective extension (if an existing serial family violence perpetrator is being sentenced for new offending); also to deferring the question of whether to make the declaration or not, if defence are seeking the opportunity to demonstrate rehabilitation.

### Consent orders

**Marriage v Tasmania Police \[2020\] TASMC 4 (6 April 2020)**

This case is actually about MPES Act. But consideres the construction of a similar "consent" provision in the context of whether it is an independent power, is still rely on a "head" provision requiring the court being satisfied as to certain matters. See para 3 onwards.

**Whether consent order requires requisite belief???**

Magistrate Jago suggested in court that the power to make an FVO under s 22 is predicated on the satisfaction of the requisite belief prescribed in s 16.

Relevant legislation:

FAMILY VIOLENCE ACT 2004 - SECT 16

16. Family violence orders

\(1\) A court may make an FVO if satisfied, on the balance of probabilities, that --

\(a\) a person has committed family violence; and

\(b\) that person may again commit family violence.

     

\(2\) An FVO may include such conditions as the court considers are necessary or desirable to prevent the commission of family violence against an affected person or to protect any other person named in the order.

     

\(3\) Without limiting the nature of the conditions which may be included in an FVO, the court may require the person against whom the FVO is to be made to do one or more of the following:

\(a\) vacate premises, not enter premises, or only enter premises on certain conditions, whether or not that person has a legal or equitable interest in the premises;

\(b\) not possess firearms specified in the order or forfeit or dispose of any firearms in his or her possession.

FAMILY VIOLENCE ACT 2004 - SECT 22

22. FVO by consent

     

\(1\) A court may make an FVO in terms consented to by the parties.

     

\(2\) In making an FVO by consent, the court may record that the person against whom the order is made does not admit any of the matters alleged in the application for that order.

Argument:

Section 16 permits the court, if satisfied on the balance of probabilities that family violence has, and is again likely to occur, to make an FVO.

Section 22 permits the court to make an FVO in terms consented to by the parties, without admission of the matters alleged in the application.

If a magistrate believes that family violence has, and is again likely to occur, the consent or otherwise of the parties is, in my mind, completely irrelevant. The court is directed by the paramount objectives of the Act to ensure the welfare of affected parties by whatever conditions necessary.

Furthermore, as a civil application, if the Magistrate has reached the requisite belief under s 16 on the balance of probabilities that family violence has, and will again occur, then the admission or otherwise of the defendant is similarly irrelevant. The court has already satisfied itself that such events will/ have occurred.

For these reasons I would suggest that s 22 must be an independent power, divorced from the requirements of s 16, allowing the court to make an FVO without the need to form the requisite belief where the terms of the application are consented to by the parties.
