14 No doubt the primary purpose of s23(4) is to facilitate proof of drink driving offences. Without s23(4) or a provision like it, difficulties could often arise in proving that a driver\'s alcohol concentration exceeded the limit at the time of his or her driving. 1 It is not possible to enumerate exhaustively circumstances which may constitute special circumstances.

2 The circumstances put forward as constituting special circumstances ought to be viewed collectively for the purpose of determining whether special circumstances exist and it is not appropriate to separately consider each matter put forward and determine whether it amounts to a special circumstance and, if not, to thereafter disregard it in the reasoning process.

3 Circumstances amounting to special circumstances may concern matters connected with the commission of the offence and matters concerning the offender.

4 Generally speaking, circumstances which may be regarded as being common to a large number of breaches of the Act or of persons who commit them do not constitute special circumstances".

24\. In *Bottomley v McDonald *[\[2002\] TASSC 27](http://www.austlii.edu.au/cgi-bin/viewdoc/au/cases/tas/TASSC/2002/27.html) Blow J, as he then was, said the following with respect to the phrase "*special circumstances*" at \[6\]:

"The expression \"special circumstances\" in [s17(5)](http://www8.austlii.edu.au/cgi-bin/viewdoc/au/legis/tas/consol_act/ta192577/s17.html) covers circumstances both of the offence and the offender. For there to be special circumstances, there must be something extraordinary, unusual or atypical. See *Dellar v Crawford *[(1992) 62 A Crim R 171](http://www.austlii.edu.au/cgi-bin/LawCite?cit=%281992%29%2062%20A%20Crim%20R%20171) at 177 - 178, and *Johnston v Davies* [\[1992\] TASSC 51](http://www.austlii.edu.au/cgi-bin/viewdoc/au/cases/tas/TASSC/1992/51.html); [(1992) 1 Tas R 183](http://www.austlii.edu.au/cgi-bin/LawCite?cit=%281992%29%201%20Tas%20R%20183) at 189. Special circumstances must be clearly distinguishable from the general run of cases that Parliament had in mind when it provided for the penalty of disqualification: *Davies v Kennedy* 81/1992 (Underwood J); *Turner v Visser* 93/1998 (Evans J). Significantly for the purposes of this case, [s17(5)](http://www8.austlii.edu.au/cgi-bin/viewdoc/au/legis/tas/consol_act/ta192577/s17.html) does not require that a period of disqualification or fine less than the normal minimum should be imposed whenever special circumstances exist. In *Johnston v Davies* (supra) at 191, Crawford J said:

\"It is to be noted that unlike the legislation considered by Bray CJ in *Baskerville v Martin *[\[1967\] SASR 156](http://www.austlii.edu.au/cgi-bin/LawCite?cit=%5b1967%5d%20SASR%20156), [s17(5)](http://www8.austlii.edu.au/cgi-bin/viewdoc/au/legis/tas/consol_act/ta192577/s17.html) does not provide that the court shall or must impose a lesser fine or lesser period of disqualification if it is satisfied that special circumstances for that course exist. What the subsection provides is that if the court is satisfied that there are special circumstances why the minimum penalty should not be imposed, the court may impose a lesser penalty. This serves to emphasise that a discretion is given to the court and notwithstanding that there may be special circumstances justifying a penalty less than the prescribed minimum, the court nevertheless has a discretion whether it should sentence accordingly. This further emphasises the need for the court to have regard to all the circumstances of the particular case, not only those relied upon by the defendant as being \"special circumstances\" but also the other circumstances which might influence the court whether or not to exercise the discretion in favour of the defendant, whether it be a very high blood alcohol reading or anything else.\"

## Requirement to get Tas Registration after 3 months

Section 28 of the Vehicle and Traffic Act states there is an exception from registration when registered under another law.

Regulation 129 of *Vehicle and Traffic (Driver Licensing and Vehicle Registration) Regulations 2021* says that the exemption lapses once in Tas for 3 months, unless they have a residential address or ordinarily reside interstate (meaning if they travel here on an extended basis they don't need to register unless the move here)
