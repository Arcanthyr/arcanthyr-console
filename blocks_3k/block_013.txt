# Absence of evidence not terminal 

***Shepperd v Cannell \[2018\] TASSC 12***

**Para 20 is a good rundown on how the failure of a police officer to have no memory of injuries is not extraordinary and not necessarily indicative that they did not exist!**

\[20\] However, there were also several aspects of the evidence upon which the magistrate was entitled to rely as supportive of the complainant\'s version. The existence of the damage within the house was important corroborative evidence. The immediate complaint made by the complainant to the police was also a matter which the magistrate was entitled to regard as supporting the consistency of the complainant\'s version. The complainant had taken photographs of injuries. While these may have provided only limited support, they were consistent with the complainant\'s version. I do not regard the evidence of the police officers, that they did not observe any injuries, as carrying significant probative weight. There was no examination of the police about the extent to which they were looking for injuries or had examined the complainant. There was no evidence of the state of the injuries, in particular the cut lip, at the time the police were present. I would not regard it as particularly exceptional that police may not have been concerned at the time of this visit with matters of forensic importance, such as making and recording observations for later use in court.

1.  This submission raises the question of the meaning of the phrase "known character proved to the court". Under the [*Evidence Act*](http://www7.austlii.edu.au/cgi-bin/viewdoc/au/legis/tas/consol_act/ea200180/), evidence of prior offences committed by the defendant could only be admitted in certain circumstances (for example, [ss97](http://www7.austlii.edu.au/cgi-bin/viewdoc/au/legis/tas/consol_act/ea200180/s97.html), [98](http://www7.austlii.edu.au/cgi-bin/viewdoc/au/legis/tas/consol_act/ea200180/s98.html) and [110](http://www7.austlii.edu.au/cgi-bin/viewdoc/au/legis/tas/consol_act/ea200180/s110.html)). If evidence was admissible pursuant to [s110(2)](http://www7.austlii.edu.au/cgi-bin/viewdoc/au/legis/tas/consol_act/ea200180/s110.html) of the [*Evidence Act*](http://www7.austlii.edu.au/cgi-bin/viewdoc/au/legis/tas/consol_act/ea200180/), to prove that the defendant was not generally a person of good character, then the hearsay rule, the opinion rule, the tendency rule and the credibility rule would not apply to such evidence. It would follow that in this circumstance, the character of the defendant could be proved on the basis of evidence which included hearsay and opinion evidence, and hence the defendant's general reputation may be relevant to this consideration. However, the preconditions for admission of such evidence under the provisions of the [*Evidence Act*](http://www7.austlii.edu.au/cgi-bin/viewdoc/au/legis/tas/consol_act/ea200180/) do not apply in this case. Rather, it is sought to have the evidence admitted by virtue of the operation of [s7(2).](http://www7.austlii.edu.au/cgi-bin/viewdoc/au/legis/tas/consol_act/ea200180/s7.html) By virtue of the provisions of [s8](http://www7.austlii.edu.au/cgi-bin/viewdoc/au/legis/tas/consol_act/ea200180/s8.html) of the [*Evidence Act*](http://www7.austlii.edu.au/cgi-bin/viewdoc/au/legis/tas/consol_act/ea200180/), that Act does not affect the operation of the provisions of any other Act. Hence, [s7](http://www7.austlii.edu.au/cgi-bin/viewdoc/au/legis/tas/consol_act/ea200180/s7.html) (2) operates independently of the [Evidence Act](http://www7.austlii.edu.au/cgi-bin/viewdoc/au/legis/tas/consol_act/ea200180/), and makes admissible evidence which would otherwise be inadmissible having regard to the provisions of the [Evidence Act](http://www7.austlii.edu.au/cgi-bin/viewdoc/au/legis/tas/consol_act/ea200180/).

99. However, [s 106](http://www.austlii.edu.au/cgi-bin/viewdoc/au/legis/tas/consol_act/ea200180/s106.html) of the [*Evidence Act*](http://www.austlii.edu.au/cgi-bin/viewdoc/au/legis/tas/consol_act/ea200180/) very significantly modifies the common law position here. Essentially [s 106](http://www.austlii.edu.au/cgi-bin/viewdoc/au/legis/tas/consol_act/ea200180/s106.html) would permit the admissibility of what would otherwise once have been termed "collateral evidence" which goes only to the credit of a witness. But such evidence is only admissible in rebuttal. That is admissible only *after* the substance of the evidence has been put to the witness and the witness has not agreed with the substance of that evidence.

100. Leave to admit that evidence is not required if the evidence "tends to prove - not "proves" of itself - that the witness has knowingly made a false representation while under an obligation to tell the truth.

101. Here the prosecution assert that the evidence of the Arunta calls is admissible without leave because it submits that the evidence here beyond those calls themselves tends to prove that the complainant lied under oath in court because of the following list of factors:

\(i\) it was accepted by the complainant that she and the defendant's father were in frequent telephone contact during the period that the defendant was remanded in custody on these matters.

\(ii\) it was accepted by the complainant that during those calls with her father-in-law they discussed her allegations against the defendant.

\(iii\) it was accepted by her that during the period that those calls were made that the complainant was aware that the defendant was also in telephone communication with his father from prison.

\(iv\) it was accepted by the complainant that in one of those calls her father-in-law did pass on a request from the defendant to her that she was to deposit funds into his prison phone account to allow him to make calls from the prison.

\(v\) it was accepted by her that she acceded to that request and did place money in that account for the defendant

\(vi\) as noted above the calls themselves in essence reveal a pattern of instructions from the defendant to his father that the complainant was to withdraw her complainant; that she make a statutory declaration to that effect; that she see a private solicitor to advance that; that she assert that she had made her complaint because her pregnancy had made her feel sick and that she was therefore moody and irritable and angry and emotional; that the defendant had never physically assaulted or significantly verbally abused or threatened her and that she had never suffered any physical injury at his hand.

\(vii\) the complainant's admitted actions between 24 October 2019 and her appearance in court to give evidence mirrored those very instructions.

102. So, crucially, even absent any concession by the complainant that she did as directed by the defendant or any other direct evidence that she agreed to do that, it was submitted that I should nevertheless admit the evidence because I do not have to be affirmatively satisfied that she did so, but rather only be satisfied that the above evidence *in toto* "tends" to establish that.

103. In my view, when considered in the light of all the relevant circumstances the evidence of the Arunta calls might well rationally cause me to so find that the complainant knowingly or reckless gave false evidence to me. The combination of making of the calls, their content, the admitted opportunity for those directions to have been conveyed to the complainant by her father in law together with the timing of the things the complainant did which match those directions in those calls so persuade me.

104. For the defence Ms Stewart submitted that I should not do so absent evidence - that is direct evidence - that conversations occurred between the complainant and the defendant's father whereby these instructions were passed on to her. I reject that for the reasons advanced by the prosecution.

105. The evidence which supports a finding that such conversations between the complainant and her father in law did occur and that she then acted in accordance with those directions is circumstantial. However, the circumstances surrounding them in sum satisfy me that such conversations occurred. In my view, it is appropriate to approach this matter as one would approach a case involving circumstantial evidence, i.e. that I should consider the combined weight of all the "strands" (i) to (vii) above (See as recently discussed by Blow CJ in *Allen v Brown* [(2016) TASSC 22).](http://www.austlii.edu.au/cgi-bin/viewdoc/au/cases/tas/TASSC/2016/22.html)

106. On that basis I admitted the evidence of the Arunta calls on the hearing as to the matter of the credit of the complainant pursuant to [s 106(2)(e).](http://www.austlii.edu.au/cgi-bin/viewdoc/au/legis/tas/consol_act/ea200180/s106.html)

## Body warn cameras:

Amendments to the *Listening Devices Act 1991* and *Police Powers (Surveillance Devices) Act 2006* -- Body Worn Cameras The Surveillance Legislation Amendments (Personal Police Cameras) Act 2018 (The Act) received Royal Assent on Friday 5 October 2018. The Act amends both the *Listening Devices Act 1991 and the Police Powers (Surveillance Devices) Act 2006*, to specifically allow for the use of personal cameras either worn, or held by police officers.

The previous legislative limitations with respect to body worn camera recordings no longer apply. Whilst members of the public may object to a recording taking place, members may use their discretion to lawfully continue an audio visual recording as necessary in the circumstances. Although advisable to do so, police officers in uniform are not required to advise members of the public that they are being recorded. The Act also amends the way in which information captured via a police personal camera can be released.

Whilst subject to normal Right to Information (RTI) and disclosure provisions, the Act introduces specific offences for inappropriately releasing or publishing 'protected information', the definition of which includes body worn camera footage. Members are encouraged to familiarise themselves with the Act, but in particular the amendment to section 33 of the *Police Powers (Surveillance Devices) Act 2006*, which is attached.
