## Opinion evidence:

Per s 79 -- a report previously written, is not "hearsay" though it is an out of court previous representation. It is evidence of an "opinion." It is not evidence of a prior rep adduced to prove an asserted fact in the representation.

### Underlying facts need not be proved:

As to the question: of whether proof of the facts and assumptions upon which expert opinion is based is a necessary precondition of admissibility under [s 79](http://www8.austlii.edu.au/cgi-bin/viewdoc/au/legis/tas/consol_act/ea200180/s79.html) of the Act and, if not, the consequences of lack of such proof on the probative value of the opinion.

\"In my view the evidence to which I have referred should be admitted. It is not adduced as evidence of the truth of the representations made to him, see [section 59](http://www8.austlii.edu.au/cgi-bin/viewdoc/au/legis/tas/consol_act/ea200180/s59.html), moreover by [section 60](http://www8.austlii.edu.au/cgi-bin/viewdoc/au/legis/tas/consol_act/ea200180/s60.html) of the [Evidence Act](http://www8.austlii.edu.au/cgi-bin/viewdoc/au/legis/tas/consol_act/ea200180/) the hearsay rule does not apply because the evidence is relevant for a non-hearsay purpose, that is, as evidence of the basis of an expert\'s opinion."

Need not prove the underlying facts upon which the opinion is based. However, proof, or lack thereof will go to weight ***Langford v Tasmania \[2018\] TASCCA 1 (18 January 2018) at para 38***

**At 44** *"... provided that the opinion is relevant, even if only tenuously so, and satisfies the conditions of admissibility set out in [s 79](http://www8.austlii.edu.au/cgi-bin/viewdoc/au/legis/cth/consol_act/ea199580/s79.html), the failure to prove underlying factual assumptions may affect the weight to be attributed to the opinion, but not its admissibility. Apart from the extreme case just discussed, weight will never affect relevance, and hence admissibility under [s 55.](http://www8.austlii.edu.au/cgi-bin/viewdoc/au/legis/cth/consol_act/ea199580/s55.html) This point was also made in IMM at \[40\]"*

## Witnesses not answering questions put to them

**[Justices Act 1959](https://www.legislation.tas.gov.au/view/html/inforce/current/act-1959-077) 43.**   **Witness not answering**

If on the appearance of a person required as a witness before justices, either voluntarily or in obedience to a summons, or upon being brought before them by virtue of a warrant, that person --

\(a\) refuses to be examined upon oath concerning the matters;

\(b\) refuses to take an oath; or

\(c\) having taken an oath refuses to answer such questions concerning the matter as are then put to him --

without offering any sufficient excuse for his refusal, any justice then present and having there jurisdiction may by warrant commit the person so refusing to a gaol, there to remain and be imprisoned for 7 days, unless in the meantime he consents to be examined and to answer concerning the matter.

A weird one:

Witness had previously spoken to police. During the conversation with the officer, she was asked about dates and details. In the cours of the conversation she sais she couldn't remmebr the exat dates, but had them in a diary. She retrieved the diary, refreshed her memoet/ read from it, and provided the police with the dates.

At the hearing, she could not remember the date, and despite lots of searching could not find the diary.

Prosecution sought to CX under 38(1)(c) \[ not clear what was inconsistent from the case\]

She was permeated to give oral evidence about the facts in the diary by virtue of s 48 in that it could not be found.

This as objected to on the previous representation, namely the saying of the dates, was based on the diary entry (itself a previous representation) and thus 2^nd^ hand hearsay.

Court allowed it, as the evidence was in the personal knowledge of the woman, and notwithstanding the reference to the diary, was fresh in the memory in the circumstances (being about a month only and a few other indicia).

It was held not to be 2^nd^ hand hearsay because her representation was not dependant upon the representation of another person, but rather her own contemporaneous notes.

She was asked about making the inconsistent statement, and agreed that she may have done so, but did not remember doing so, or the specific dates. The requirements of s 43 were also addressed. Having put the representation to her, and getting the answer "could not remember", it enlivened s 106, and evidence oculd be tendered through the officer she talked to, that she did so, and the specific dates she told him could be tendered. As it is in through 106, s60 gets it in as proof of the fact.

## Leave under s192 (s 26)

### Used to curtail defence questioning

**Police v Endlay \[2020\] TASMC 13 (9 January 2020) \[92-\]**

92. the *[Evidence Act](http://www.austlii.edu.au/cgi-bin/viewdoc/au/legis/tas/consol_act/ea200180/), *[s 26](http://www.austlii.edu.au/cgi-bin/viewdoc/au/legis/tas/consol_act/ea200180/s26.html) gives the Court power to disallow certain forms of questioning. In my view this is such an example. It provides as follows:

*"26. **Court\'s control over questioning of witness***

*The court may make any orders it considers just in relation to --*

*(a) the way in which witnesses are to be questioned; and*

*(b) the production and use of documents and things in connection with the questioning of witnesses; and*

*(c) the order in which parties may question a witness; and*

*(d) the presence and behaviour of any person in connection with the questioning of witnesses."*

93. [Section 26](http://www.austlii.edu.au/cgi-bin/viewdoc/au/legis/tas/consol_act/ea200180/s26.html) gives the Court very considerable breadth of powers to ensure a fair and efficient hearing. In *ASIC v Rich* [(2006) 201 FLR 206](http://www.austlii.edu.au/cgi-bin/LawCite?cit=%282006%29%20201%20FLR%20206), Austin J held that in the exercise of the power it has under [s 26](http://www.austlii.edu.au/cgi-bin/viewdoc/au/legis/tas/consol_act/ea200180/s26.html) that the Courts should consider the factors listed in [s 192](http://www.austlii.edu.au/cgi-bin/viewdoc/au/legis/tas/consol_act/ea200180/s192.html) of the act.

94. [Section 192](http://www.austlii.edu.au/cgi-bin/viewdoc/au/legis/tas/consol_act/ea200180/s192.html) provides that:

*"192. **Leave, permission or direction may be given on terms***

*(1) If, because of this Act, a court may give any leave, permission or direction, the leave, permission or direction may be given on any terms the court thinks fit.*

*(2) Without limiting the matters that the court may take into account in deciding whether to give the leave, permission or direction, it is to take into account --*

*(a) the extent to which to do so would be likely to add unduly to, or to shorten, the length of the hearing; and*

*(b) the extent to which to do so would be unfair to a party or to a witness; and*

*(c) the importance of the evidence in relation to which the leave, permission or direction is sought; and*

*(d) the nature of the proceeding; and*

*(e) the power of the court, if any, to adjourn the hearing or to make another order or to give a direction in relation to the evidence."*

95. In my view, s 192(2)(a) and (b) are of relevance here. In that regard I briefly refer to *ASIC v Citigroup Global Markets Australia Pty Ltd* [(2007) FCA 121](http://www.austlii.edu.au/cgi-bin/viewdoc/au/cases/cth/FCA/2007/121.html) at par7 and 8.
