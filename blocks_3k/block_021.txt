### Contra section 12 surety arguments:

R v Barrett is a 1985 SA decision referring to the 1843 Uk judgment of the queen v badger. It is a common law principle applied in the context of a presumption in favour of bail

Neither contemplated or considered the introduction of the Family violence act. However violent a departure from common law principles it may be, Section 12 is binding. The court must be satisfied as to the safety and wellbeing of the complainant. The surety's capacity to, pay, alone, should the defendant act contrary to that requirement, is inherently insufficient to discharge that requirement. It is the "supervision" of a defendant by proposed surety that can satisfy the court's, and in that question, the character and antecedents of the proposed surety are not only relevant, but necessary considerations. At para 14 of ***Tasmania v Wooders \[2022\] TASSC 15***

*A person who provides a recognizance has an important role, not to be entered into lightly. He or she is, unlike the Court, in a position to supervise obedience to the order and the conditions. A surety undertakes a real and substantial obligation to ensure that the person admitted to bail appears when required and complies with the conditions, and must understand the obligations of the accused and the consequences of breach.*

\[15\] *Secondly, the recognizance also serves to motivate the person released on bail to comply by the assurance that, if they fail to appear or breach a condition, the person who agreed to provide the recognizance will suffer the forfeiture. The accused must know that, if he or she breaks the trust that has been placed in them by a surety, often a family member or friend, the surety will suffer: Mokbel v DPP. The object is to dissuade an accused from breach because of the adverse consequences which might otherwise flow to a surety.*

#### Unfairness is a part-and-parcel of being a surety:

Cited in **Tasmania v Wooders \[2022\]).**

In R v Crown Court at Maidenstone; ex parte Lever \[1995\] 2 All ER 35, Hoffman LJ said at 41:

*\"It follows that in one sense the system has unfairness built into it. It may result in persons entirely innocent having to suffer on account of the wrongdoing of another. # Mental health treatment order

Two types

Ones from the court process and ones for civilians (who might incidentally have court matters at or about the time)

For civilians, people can be taken into protective custody (pursuant to s17 MH Act) by a cop or medical officer for an "assessment" order. They have to be assessed within about 24 hours of appearing at the centre and can then be held for up to 72 hours (per Mental Health Act).

They can then be held under a "treatment order" for up to 6 months if found appropriate by the mental health tribunal. However, they are subject to 60 day reviews.
