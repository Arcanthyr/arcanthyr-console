\[86\]T... The foundation of criminal responsibility under s 4 is the \"mutual embarkation on a crime\". The section establishes the ambit of criminal responsibility by participants while carrying out a common unlawful purpose. In contrast to the common law, the Code defines this ambit by requiring a connection of objective probability between the common purpose and the committed crime.

\[83\] *Interpreted in this way, criminal responsibility will arise when:*

*(a) two or more persons form a common intention to prosecute an unlawful purpose in conjunction with one another, and*

*(b) a crime is committed in the prosecution of such unlawful purpose, and*

*(c) the crime committed is of such a nature that its commission is a probable consequence of the prosecution of the unlawful purpose.*

*83 Provided the requisite connection is established between the unlawful purpose and the crime, there is nothing in the text of the provision which suggests that its operation should be limited by excluding crimes which are included within the intended scope of the unlawful purpose. Obviously, the provision extends the ambit of responsibility beyond this, by applying an objective test of probable consequence. However, this prescribes the outer reach of the provision. It does not expressly or impliedly exclude intended crimes. Self-evidently, a crime which is intended will meet the objective test of \"probable consequence\".*

\[87} "...The criminal responsibility of the person should be determined by application of the test prescribed by s 4 according to its terms. Under that test, a person will be responsible for any crime committed in the prosecution of the common purpose, provided that the crime is of such a nature that its commission was a probable consequence of such prosecution, including those where the test is satisfied because the crime was expressly agreed or contemplated by the common purpose. The intention of the provision is to attribute criminal responsibility to all such crimes, on the basis of their connection to the prosecution of the unlawful purpose, irrespective of the contribution actually made by the person as defined in s 3. The section defines the limits of criminal responsibility arising from the prosecution of common purpose. There is no justification for excluding crimes which fall within that ambit from attribution of criminal responsibility."

100 As already noted, a consequence of the wider interpretation is the potential for considerable overlap between ss 3 and 4, because in cases in which the crime committed is that which is intended by the participants, criminal responsibility will almost certainly arise under both provisions. We see no difficulty either in principle or in practical application with this outcome. In fact, we would consider it to be the expected and appropriate effect of provisions which attribute criminal responsibility to those complicit in a crime on more than one basis. Certainly, under s 3 it is common for criminal responsibility to arise under more than one provision. We accept that there is a practical danger that relying on both provisions in every case to which both may apply on the facts, could lead to unnecessarily complicated directions, and thereby create confusion for juries. Any such problem is capable of amelioration by the exercise of appropriate discretion on the part of the prosecution. As a matter of practice, resort should only be had by prosecutors to s 4 in cases where, on the facts, there may realistically be a need to rely on the extended ambit of the provision in order to attribute criminal responsibility, or where there is clear evidence of a preformed common unlawful purpose, which is additional to inferences drawn from the conduct of the parties in the commission of the crime. In any event, practical issues concerning the potential for overlap of the provisions will not \"deny their separate utility\".
