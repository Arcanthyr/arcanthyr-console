### POLICE POWERS OF SEARCH AND SEIZURE

***Scope of the search warrant (i.e. seizing items not particularised in the warrant). *** 

Police can't use a warrant to go on a fishing exercise: ***Crowley v Murphy*.**

  

However, whether they can lawfully seize items not mentioned in the warrant will depend on whether the conditions from ***Ghani v Jones***, which are now embodied (mostly) in s. 6 of the *Search Warrants Act (tas)*, can be satisfied: 

1.  Must relate to a serious offence; 

<!-- -->

2.  Property must be the fruity of the crime or the means by which it was committed, or material evidence of its commission; and 

<!-- -->

3.  That the person in possession was involved in the commission of the crime either as a principal or accessory.  

In addition: 

4.  Police must keep them for no longer that necessary to complete investigations or to preserve as evidence; and 

<!-- -->

5.  The lawfulness of the police conduct must be judged at the time of the seizure, not by what happens later (hindsight or subsequent events cannot justify the seizure).  

Under s6(e) of the *Search Warrants Act* the executing officer can seize any material found on the premises that they believe on reasonable grounds to be either: 

- Evidential material in relation to the offence which the warrant relates; or

- Evidential material in relation to another offence.

### 3.3 Common law rights

The only time that search and seizure is permitted is when pursuant to arrest; pursuant to a warrant or with consent.

***Coco v R*** (1994) 179 CLR 427 at pp 435-6 per Mason CJ, Brennan, Gaudron and McHugh JJ

***"Every unauthorized entry upon private property is a trespass, the right of a person in possession or entitled to possession of premises to exclude others from those premises being a fundamental common law right."*** (emphasis added) @ 435

#### Search and seizure: common law and statute

A search is an examination of a person or property and a seizure is a taking of what is discovered. Seizure under the Fourth Amendment to the United States Constitution also extends to seizure of persons which in New Zealand might more readily \[\*33\] be dealt with under the arbitrary arrest and detention provisions of s 22.

The common law does not recognise a right to search anyone in the absence of an arrest. Nor does it recognise a general right of search on arrest. As an incident to an arrest a police officer may search an accused and take possession of any property tending to show that person\'s guilt (Barnett & Grant v Campbell (1902) 21 NZLR 484). As well as searching for such property the police may search for objects with which a person might do himself or herself or any others injury, or which might be employed to facilitate escape. As Donaldson LJ observed in Lindley v Rutter (1980) 72 Cr App R 1, 6:

\"It is the duty of any constable who lawfully has a prisoner in his charge to take all reasonable measures to ensure that the prisoner does not escape or assist others to do so, does not injure himself or others, does not destroy or dispose of evidence and does not commit further crime such, for example, as malicious damage to property. This list is not exhaustive, but it is sufficient for present purposes. What measures are reasonable in the discharge of this duty will depend upon the likelihood that the particular \[\*34\] prisoner will do any of these things unless prevented. That in turn will involve the constable in considering the known or apparent disposition and sobriety of the prisoner. What can never be justified is the adoption of any particular measures without regard to all the circumstances of the particular case.\"

A personal search is a restraint on freedom and an affront to human dignity. It is exercisable at common law only for good reason. The Courts will examine any claimed justification with great care and generally speaking no one should be required to submit to that restraint unless he or she knows in substance the reason for the search (Brazil v Chief Constable of Surrey \[1983\] 1 WLR 1155, 1162).

Special statutes authorise personal including body searches in particular cases and s 57A of the Police Act 1958 adds to the common law right of search on arrest. It allows for the search and seizure of all money and all or any other property found on or in the possession of a person taken into lawful custody and at a police station or in any other premises or vehicle used for police purposes.

The common law did not allow any entry and search of private property by officers of the \[\*35\] state without permission of the owner or occupier except under a warrant issued by a justice acting on evidence that there was strong cause to believe that stolen goods were concealed there.

#### When can you search at common law?

A District Court Judge, justice or Registrar may issue a search warrant if satisfied that there is reasonable ground for believing that there is present there:

\(a\) Any thing upon or in respect of which any offence punishable by imprisonment has been or is suspected of having been committed; or

\(b\) Any thing which there is reasonable ground to believe will be evidence as to the commission of any such of-fence; or

\(c\) Any thing which there is reasonable ground to believe is intended to be used for the purpose of committing any such offence . . .

Under other specific statutes conditions under which warrants may be issued or warrantless searches undertaken are expressed in quite different ways and there is no single test or criterion running through the legislation.

#### When is a search \"unreasonable\"

A search will be unreasonable if the circumstances giving rise to the search make the search itself unreasonable or if an otherwise initially reasonable search is carried out in an unreasonable manner.

Section 21 is a negative provision in that it is a restraint on governmental action and confers no powers on the government. In particular it does not em-power a \"reasonable\" search.

The test of unreasonableness requires consideration of the values underlying the right and a balancing of the relevant values and public interests involved. As apparent from Entick v Carrington, where the action was for trespass to property, the common law concern was for the protections of property rights: entry on to private property was a trespass and the seizure of property was a conversion. However both in America and Canada privacy values have become an accepted basis for the constitutional protection.

### 3.4 Grounds of the Search: Reasonable Grounds to Suspect or Believe

#### What is reasonable suspicion?

***Lindley v Rutter*** \[1981\] QB 128

A drunken woman was taken to a police station where her bra was taken off. She was arrested -- resisted search -- two officers searched her believing they were under a standing order to search every female prisoner and remove the bra for their own protection.

There is a duty of an officer to have a lawful purpose for search -- a standing order from a senior officer is not enough. There has to be a basis.

Held: If they officer's mind did not apply itself to having a lawful purpose for the search, than the search was unlawful (subjective mindset of officer required level of analysis). There was no legal basis to search her -- standing order not sufficient.

***George v Rockett*** (1990) 170 CLR 104

- *Reasonable suspicion is: the existence of facts which are sufficient to induce that state of mind in a reasonable person.' @ 112*

- *Suspicion is 'a state of conjecture or surmise where proof is lacking' @ 115*

- *'The facts which can reasonably ground a suspicion may be quite insufficient reasonably to ground a belief, yet some factual basis for the suspicion must be shown.' @ 115. (George v Rockett) -- so suspicion is less than a belief.*

See 111 of judgement. Reference and description of the search is the basis of est the boundaries the search powers. There must be specificity in the warrant -- the type of evidence soiught must be detailed

***\*Nguyen v Elliott*** (unreported, Supreme Court, Victoria, Hedigan J, No 7540 of 1994, 6 February 1995)

(Note Tasmanian has a higher standard of reasonable belief than this Vic example)

The act of a suspect kicking out and contacting a police officer , is not out of proportion to the threat of being searched against ones will, and that any bruising which may follow is justified in the circumstances where the it is an unwarranted search.

The subsequent charge for actions following an arrest on this basis is not grounds upon which the def can be held accountable. One cannot be guilty of resiting officer in course of duty when an unwarranted search is clearly not in the course of an officer's duty.

[***\*Ghani v Jones*** \[1970\] 1 QB 693]{.mark}

This case represents the 'high water mark'

**Held**. Per Lord Denning: it is settled law that where the police enter premises pursuant to search warrant or pursuant to power of arrest in relation to serious offence; they are entitled to search for and take any goods in relation to that offence. If during the search they find other goods relating to other events they are entitled to seize them as well provided they behave reasonably and detain the goods for no longer than is necessary.

Fivefold test of Lord Denning MR:

***Can seize item when legally on the premises, without the consent of the owner if:***

1\. Reasonable grounds for believing serious offence has been committed

2\. Reasonable grounds for believing the article in question is either the fruit of the crime, the instrument by which the crime is committed, or is material evidence

- Great Train Robbery -- a saucer of milk given to a cat on a farm where the robbers hid

3\. Reasonable grounds to believe that the person in possession of it has himself committed the crime, or is implicated, or is accessory or at any rate his refusal must be quite unreasonable

4\. Must not keep the article nor prevents its removal for any longer than is reasonably necessary to complete their investigations or to preserve it for evidence. If a copy will suffice, a copy.

5\. The lawfulness of the conduct of the police will be judged at the time and not by what happens afterwards

***\*Jeffrey v Black*** \[1977\] 3 WLR 895

Sandwich case: Police officers may not search premises of an arrestee without either a warrant or the consent of the accused, at least for evidence of matters unrelated to the reason for the previous arrest of the accused. There must be a connection with the evidence gathered/ search and the initial charge.

If related the reason for the previous arrest -- tickety boo.

So if search granted, they can search for and take things relating to that, but are not authorised to search for evidence which is unrelated to the offence.

Silent is not consent either.

Case also stands for the principle that whee there is a serious crime; the suspect can have their premises searched -- not for misdemeanours.

They ignored the right of the sandwich thief to refuse entry.

***\*Tasmania v Woodberry*** \[2012\] TASSC 89

Serious offence where woman stole \$30,000 from business she worked for. Visited by 2 police officers. Not cautioned or warned that she could speak to a lawyer so there was an arrest.

She was taken from her place of business and was told that she would need to accompany them to the house -- she claimed that she was compelled to comply with the search.

The court noted that there was no power of the police to compel her to submit to a search of her house -- in so doing, the police officer ***disregarded her right to refuse entry***. All evidence of the search was excluded. There were other means available to the officers to initiate the search such as consent or getting a search warrant.
