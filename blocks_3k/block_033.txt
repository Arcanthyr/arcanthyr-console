## Passive ingestion of Meth

Miriam Connor from FFST in an email dated 29 May 2018:

Methylamphetamine is heated to become a vapour which is then actively breathed into the lungs. It does not exist in the air, like smoke from cigarettes or cannabis -- therefore it cannot be inhaled passively. There is no scientific literature or research into passive methylamphetamine exposure, as it is just not possible to passively inhale methylamphetamine vapour, from our understanding. It will be difficult to write a proof for this, given there is no scientific evidence, but the physical characteristics of methylamphetamine mean that it does not exist in a gas form, it has to be heated to form a vapour.

Further to this, the only other information that I believe could be of help to you is that methylamphetamine hydrochloride (commonly referred to as crystal meth) the form typically "smoked" has a melting point of between 170-175 degrees. Obviously it will vaporise above this temperature but this gives an indication that methamphetamine is not just floating around in the air of a room at ambient temperature when people are using it.  It will, in fact, easily condense on the cooler parts of the glass smoking devices used before it has any chance of "escaping" into a room -- at any significant/detectable level. Meth and THC differ considerably in how they are "smoked". Cannabis is typically literally burnt as per a cigarette so that the smoke is inhaled/exhaled. Meth is heated until it changes from a solid to a liquid to a gas and this is what is inhaled . Smoke from a cannabis joint can be long lasting (given the right conditions e.g. lack of ventilation), meth vapour is not the same.

## Required to inform driver of requirement 

**McLean v Wilkie \[2022\] TASSC 13 (24 February 2022)**

Need to communicate requirement. Failure to do so makes ait an unlawful arrest. But evidence may still get in under s138,

\[11\] \"The defendant objected to the admissibility of the result of the breath analysis on the basis that Constable Prettyman (sic), had unlawfully arrested him and failed to communicate to the defendant that he was required \'*\...to proceed to such place or into such vehicle\... as the officer may indicate and there submit himself \... to that analysis\...\'* as contemplated by the *Road *Safety* Alcohol and Drugs) Act 1970*, s 10(1) (the Act). The officer was also obliged to communicate the reason for the obligation. I adopted the reasoning of Wood J in *Arnesto v Hickman*[^\[1\]^](http://www.austlii.edu.au/cgi-bin/viewdoc/au/cases/tas/TASSC/2022/13.html#fn1)* *as to the meaning of the word *\'require\' *in the [*Police Offences Act 1935*](http://www.austlii.edu.au/cgi-bin/viewdoc/au/legis/tas/consol_act/poa1935140/), [s55A](http://www.austlii.edu.au/cgi-bin/viewdoc/au/legis/tas/consol_act/poa1935140/s55a.html) as logically applicable to the same expression* *in the Act, s 10(1).

## Refuse to submit to blood test

Needle phobia no excuse.

See para 23-33 ***Lyons v Dyson*** \[2018\] TASMC

In summary, the magistrate found that satisfying the court that the defendant had a needle phobia is insufficient, he needed to satisfy the court that he had the needle phobia, and that the taking of a sample of blood would have been detrimental to his physical or mental health and that appropriate interventions (such as being given a sedative to calm him) could not be used to enable the sample to be taken.
